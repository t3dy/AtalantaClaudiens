1. Audio recordings of Atalanta fugiens
Known modern commercial or complete‑cycle recordings:

Ensemble Plus Ultra, dir. Michael Noone, “Maier: Atalanta Fugiens” (50 fugues; ca. 71 minutes). Issued 2011 (Claudio/Apple Music, also on Spotify and other platforms).

Claudio Records issue “The 50 Fugues of Atalanta Fugiens – Michael Maier – CR5468” (with booklet notes by Joscelyn Godwin; the label’s page is often cited as the main modern recording reference).
​

RIM c‑Orchestra / David Kanaga, “Atalanta Fugiens 1–18” (electronic/experimental re‑imagining of fugues 1–18, released via Bandcamp, 2021).
​

Camerata Mediolanense, albums titled “Atalanta Fugiens” and “Atalanta Fugiens (Deluxe Edition)” (a free creative work inspired by Maier rather than a straight liturgical‑style realization of the 50 canons).
​

These, plus scattered tracks and partial realizations on streaming platforms, are as close as I can get to a discography of recordings explicitly marketed as Atalanta fugiens.

Godwin‑related recordings
Claudio Records’ “The 50 Fugues of Atalanta Fugiens – Michael Maier – CR5468” includes a program note by Joscelyn Godwin and is usually taken as the “Godwin” realization you have in mind, even though the performers are Ensemble Plus Ultra under Michael Noone.

Godwin’s English translation (Phanes Press, 1989, Magnum Opus Hermetic Sourceworks 22) includes modern notation of the music, which has been the basis for many later performances and digital realizations.
​

Furnace and Fugue: A Digital Edition of Michael Maier’s Atalanta fugiens (1618) with Scholarly Commentary (Brown University / University of Virginia Press, 2020). The edition presents each emblem with a modern digital recording of its three‑voice canon.

5. Scholarly bibliography on Atalanta fugiens
The core modern scholarly literature (partial but representative) is:

H. M. E. de Jong, Michael Maier’s Atalanta Fugiens: Sources of an Alchemical Book of Emblems (Leiden: E. J. Brill, 1969). Foundational monograph analyzing sources and structure.

H. M. E. de Jong, modern critical edition and English translation (published 2002) of Atalanta fugiens, providing text, notes, and contextual information; the RIDE review notes that Furnace and Fugue builds on De Jong’s 1969 study and her 2002 edition.
​

H. M. E. de Jong, various articles on Maier and Atalanta in journal literature; these are summarized but not fully listed in the RIDE review.
​

Hofmeier edition (2007) of Atalanta fugiens (with abundant indices), cited as an important source for Furnace and Fugue.
​

Tara Nummedal and Donna Bilak (eds.), Furnace and Fugue: A Digital Edition of Michael Maier’s “Atalanta fugiens” (1618) with Scholarly Commentary (Brown University Digital Publications / University of Virginia Press, 2020). This includes multiple essays that treat images, music, and textual commentary from different disciplinary angles.

Articles and book chapters on “alchemy and music” that include sections on Maier’s book; the RIDE review notes that “a number of publications revolving around the topic of ‘alchemy and music’ exist, most of which at least mention Maier’s Atalanta fugiens,” and specifically cites Rola (1988), Gaudio (2020), and Wagner (2021) among emblem‑studies contributions that engage with Maier.
​

Earlier emblem‑studies and alchemy‑studies work, such as Figala and Neumann’s characterization of Atalanta as “one of the most beautiful books of alchemical literature of all time,” often cited as a touchstone description.
​
