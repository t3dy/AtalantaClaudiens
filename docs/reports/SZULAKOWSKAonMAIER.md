## 1. Szulakowska's Positioning of Maier

Szulakowska situates Maier (1568-1622) within a triad of late-Renaissance Paracelsian alchemists — Khunrath, Maier, and Fludd — who collectively transformed alchemical illustration from medieval Christological imagery into a new classicizing visual language. She argues that Maier's emblems in the *Atalanta Fugiens* (1617/1618) "set the style for alchemical illustration for the next three centuries, replacing the earlier Christological motifs" (*Sacrificial Body*, p. 36). Fludd and Mylius subsequently copied and adapted Maier's visual innovations, with Mylius reproducing AF emblems in detail in his *Philosophia Reformata* (1622).

Szulakowska identifies Maier as a transitional figure: a Lutheran whose religious convictions were "so reticent" that he "disguised his religious references in barely decipherable signifiers" (*Sacrificial Body*, p. 70). Unlike Khunrath, who openly identified Christ with the philosopher's stone, Maier approached Christological themes through layers of classical and alchemical allegory.

## 2. The Alchemical Mass (Symbola Aureae Mensae)

Szulakowska's most sustained analysis of Maier concerns the alchemical mass illustration from the *Symbola Aureae Mensae Duodecim Nationum* (1617), which she examines in both books. Maier reprinted the 15th-century treatise of Nicolaus Melchior Cibinensis (*Processus sub forma missae*) and commissioned Matthaeus Merian to engrave an accompanying illustration of the Virgin Mary as the Apocalyptic Woman from Revelation 12.

Szulakowska argues that Maier's illustration is "a far more radical disquisition on the alchemical mass than Cibinensis' discrete original text" (*Sacrificial Body*, p. 57). Where Cibinensis had cautiously discontinued his account before the consecration of bread and wine, Maier's engraving "clearly identifies Christ with the 'lapis philosophorum' and the elixir of life" — a bold exposition of the alchemical process as a re-enactment, not merely a symbolization, of the Catholic Communion.

In *The Alchemical Virgin Mary*, Szulakowska connects this image to the "Turkish Madonna" tradition and to Emperor Rudolf II's military campaigns against the Ottomans. She notes that Maier served Rudolf as physician and imperial counsellor, was elevated to Count Palatine, and published the *Symbola* after Rudolf's death in 1612 — possibly as a tribute to his former patron's victories.

## 3. Atalanta Fugiens: Specific Emblems Discussed

Szulakowska discusses several AF emblems in detail, always within the framework of bodily transformation, Eucharistic symbolism, and the politics of the Protestant Reformation:

### Emblem XIX — The Four Warriors
Szulakowska reads the four interconnected warriors (four elements whose death is mutual) as a Christological allegory: "when this dead one arises, then death, darkness and the waters of the abyss shall flee from him." She identifies Maier's reference to Hermes testifying that "our Son shall live and the dead King shall come out of the fire" as a disguised reference to Christ's resurrection, arguing that Maier mitigates the physical violence of dismemberment emblems while retaining their salvific meaning (*Sacrificial Body*, pp. 69-70).

### Emblem XXIV — The Wolf Devouring the King
The wolf (antimony) devours the King (Saturn/prime matter), then the wolf is calcinated, and the king returns to life "in a young and vigorous form as the tincture that cures every disease." Szulakowska traces this allegory to Basil Valentine's emblem of the wolf, connecting it to the broader theme of alchemical dismemberment and resurrection (*Sacrificial Body*, p. 70).

### Emblem XXVIII — King Duenech in the Steam Bath
Szulakowska connects this emblem to the alchemical understanding of bodily purification through the humoral system. King Duenech's steam bath frees him from black bile (*aqua foetida*), the cause of saturnine melancholia. She notes that "alchemists used baths in order to improve the proportions of the humours in their chemicals, to produce healthy blood in the body and to convert cold and moist materials into warm and dry ones" (*Sacrificial Body*, p. 69). Tilton's identification of the source as the Allegory of Merlin is cited.

### Emblem XXXV — Digestion and Feeding
Szulakowska discusses Maier's treatment of digestion as "an indispensable stage in alchemical practice, comparable to the feeding of a young child and to agricultural processes" (*Sacrificial Body*, p. 68). She connects this to the broader alchemical rehabilitation of bodily functions that wider culture treated as taboo.

### Emblem XLIV — Belinus/Osiris Resurrection
The figure of Belinus, a late Hellenistic version of Osiris drawn from the *Rosarium Philosophorum*, appears as a crowned figure rising from a coffin while his body is dismembered in the background. Szulakowska quotes the text: "when you have taken me partly out of my nature, and my wife out of hers and after you have killed these natures and we are raised by a new and incorporeal resurrection, so that after that we cannot die any more" (*Sacrificial Body*, p. 70).

### Emblem XL — The Two Fountains / Elixir of Life
In this emblem, two fountains allude to the elixir of life described by Raymund Lull. Szulakowska notes that Maier states the elixir's meaning is "analogous to the water of life of Christ, meaning by this both the sacrament of Baptism and also that of the Eucharist" (*Sacrificial Body*, p. 70). She identifies this as one of the "slightly more overt references to the alchemical sacrament" in AF, as opposed to Maier's usually highly coded religious symbolism.

## 4. Maier and the Rosarium Philosophorum

In *The Alchemical Virgin Mary*, Szulakowska draws on De Jong's research to situate Maier's AF within the tradition of the *Rosarium Philosophorum* (Basel, 1550). She writes that De Jong "has revealed" that Maier "was basing himself substantially on the Rosarium (as well as on the Aurora Consurgens and the much older Turba Philosophorum)" — the same sources that contained Marian and Christological imagery that Maier transformed into his classicizing emblems (*Alchemical Virgin Mary*, p. 46).

The alchemical marriage in the Rosarium tradition involved an incestuous coupling of brother and sister (Sol and Luna), who then "died" and putrefied — the same sequence Maier develops across multiple AF emblems (IV, XXX, XXXIII, XXXVIII). Szulakowska notes that this alchemical coniunctio drew on Marian theology through the Rosarium's mediation.

## 5. Maier's Religious Positioning

Szulakowska's most distinctive contribution to Maier scholarship is her argument that Maier, Khunrath, and Fludd regarded their chemical procedures "as being essentially the same rite as the mass" — a "symbolic usurpation of the highest spiritual and political authority since the right to offer Communion was jealously guarded" (*Sacrificial Body*, p. 25). She frames this in the context of the Protestant Reformation's battles over the Eucharist, arguing that Paracelsian alchemists developed their own version of sacramental practice through chemical operations.

Maier's generation (post-1590s) replaced explicit Christological imagery with classical and Hermetic allegory, but the underlying sacramental structure persisted. Szulakowska argues that Maier's *Atalanta Fugiens* represents this transition: the classical mythological surface (Atalanta, Hippomenes, Osiris, Oedipus) conceals a fundamentally Eucharistic understanding of alchemical transformation.

## 6. Maier's Biographical Context

From both books, Szulakowska provides biographical context:
- Maier trained as a physician and philosopher
- Served Emperor Rudolf II as doctor and imperial counsellor
- Elevated to hereditary nobility and granted the title of Count Palatine
- Left Rudolf's service in April 1611
- Published his major alchemical works from 1617 onward, after Rudolf's death (1612)
- Spent time in England (arrived early 1612), attached to the court until 1618
- Engaged in probable dialogue with Robert Fludd during his English stay
- Sent a Christmas card to James I constructed in the shape of a rose (cited as evidence of Rosicrucian convictions)
- Wrote supportive commentary on the Rosicrucian Manifestos in the *Themis Aurea* (1618)
- Defended Paracelsus against Erastus in *De Circulo Physico, Quadrato* (1616)
- Dedicated a whole treatise to potable gold, arguing gold was "the sun in the material sphere"

## 7. Relevance to AtalantaClaudiens

Szulakowska offers a dimension absent from De Jong's source-critical approach: the **religious and political context** of Maier's emblems. Where De Jong traces each emblem to its alchemical textual sources, Szulakowska traces the same imagery to Reformation theology, Eucharistic controversy, and the political dynamics of the Habsburg court. Her readings are complementary:

| Emblem | De Jong's Reading | Szulakowska's Addition |
|--------|------------------|----------------------|
| XIX (Four Warriors) | Elemental unity, Geryon myth | Christological resurrection allegory |
| XXIV (Wolf/King) | Antimony refining, Basil Valentine | Alchemical dismemberment as sacrificial body theology |
| XXVIII (Steam Bath) | Allegoria Merlini, humoral medicine | Purification as Eucharistic cleansing |
| XXXV (Digestion) | Agricultural analogy, feeding | Rehabilitation of bodily functions against Reformation taboo |
| XL (Two Fountains) | Lullian elixir | Explicit Baptism/Eucharist dual reference |
| XLIV (Osiris) | Egyptian myth, Rosarium | Belinus as Christ-figure, incorporeal resurrection |
