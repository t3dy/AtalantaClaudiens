Alchemical Toad FIGURE

Bufo

The toad occupies a peculiar place in both natural history and folk belief. Cold, warty, earth-dwelling, and popularly believed to be poisonous, the toad was regarded in medieval and early modern culture as a creature of the lowest order, associated with darkness, venom, and the subterranean world. Yet it was also credited with concealing a precious jewel in its head (the toadstone or bufonite), a belief Shakespeare exploits in As You Like It. This conjunction of repulsive exterior and hidden treasure made the toad an irresistible figure for alchemical writers seeking to express the paradox that the Philosophers' Stone is found in the most despised and lowly of substances.

In the alchemical tradition, the toad represents the fixed, earthy, dry aspect of the prima materia. Abraham identifies the toad with the philosophical matter or child that must be nourished with mercurial milk during the cibation. The toad is synonymous with the crow and the dragon in Jonson's Alchemist, where Surly lists "your toade, your crow, your dragon, and your panther" as interchangeable symbols. The toad is frequently paired with the eagle: the earthly toad bound to the flying eagle represents the sublimation of fixed matter by the volatile spirit. Stolcius inscribes this formula directly: "Join together an earthly toad and a flying eagle."

Maier makes the toad the disturbing centrepiece of Emblem V, whose motto instructs: "Put a toad to the breasts of a woman, that she may feed it, and the woman may die, and the toad grows big from the milk." The engraved plate depicts a woman nursing a large toad at her breast while a walled town rises behind her. The discourse develops the image with unsettling detail: the woman feeds the toad with her milk, and the toad's poison enters her veins, killing her as Cleopatra was killed by the asp. The toad is the woman's son, and therefore must naturally be fed by her milk. Maier then tells the story from Guilielmus Novobrigensis of a living toad with a gold chain found inside a quarried stone, locked away again by a bishop who feared a bad omen. Maier sneers at this literal account but recognizes the allegorical truth: a humble creature adorned with gold, imprisoned in darkness, is the Philosophers' Stone.

De Jong traces the motto of Emblem V to the Pseudo-Aristotelian Tractatulus de Practica Lapidis Philosophici, which describes the preparation of the Stone in four stages. The woman suckling the toad represents the third stage, where the purified, very dry earth must be made pliable by adding water. The toad, representing the element earth, grows as it absorbs the water, and the woman, representing the element water, dies. De Jong cites Lullius' comparison of the suckling of the Stone with the suckling of a child at the mother's breast: the child (toad) coagulates and grows while the mother (water) dissolves. The formula is precise: coagulation of one nature cannot occur without the dissolution of another. The toad and the woman are son and mother, and the whole substance of the feeder transforms into food for the fed.

Emblem V is among the most visually arresting and emotionally disturbing images in the Atalanta Fugiens, and Maier clearly intended it to be so. The reader must overcome revulsion to grasp the chemical logic: the dry earth must absorb moisture to become workable, and this absorption exhausts the liquid. The toad's grotesque feeding is the cibation, the nourishing of the Stone. Maier's lusus serius transforms a laboratory procedure into a tableau of maternal sacrifice and monstrous filial appetite, daring the reader to find the noble medicine hidden in the poison.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The earthly counter-pole to the airy eagle: the terra damnata or fixed faecal residue at the bottom of the alembic, whose stubborn weight is what the volatile spirit must return to and animate.
Medical
The melancholic deposit of the body—black bile sediment, sluggish humor—that resists circulation; the toad-like residue medicine must dissolve and reincorporate rather than discard.
Spiritual
The repulsive but indispensable shadow-matter of the psyche; the ugly, low, unwanted material that contemplation must befriend, since the eagle without the toad cannot fly home.
Cosmological
The earth-element in its densest aspect: the chthonic principle whose toad-emblem signals the deep, cool, moist substrate from which all generation proceeds.
