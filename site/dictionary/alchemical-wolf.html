Alchemical Wolf FIGURE

Lupus

The wolf occupies a singular place in medieval and early modern natural history as a creature of legendary voracity. Pliny and other classical authorities described the wolf as so ravenous that, failing to find prey, it would devour earth itself, growing so heavy it could hardly be knocked over. The wolf was sacred to Apollo and Latona in classical mythology: according to legend, Latona could not have given birth without the wolf's protective presence, and wolves' eyes were said to radiate light in the darkness. These associations with consumption, purification through destruction, and nocturnal illumination made the wolf an ideal vehicle for alchemical allegory.

In the alchemical tradition, the wolf is antimony, known as lupus metallorum, the wolf of metals. Abraham identifies the wolf with the ferocious devouring power of antimony, which consumes impure metallic bodies in order to purify them. The wolf is synonymous with the mercurial waters of life and death, the universal solvent that dissolves metals, then cleanses, purifies, and regenerates them. Basil Valentine, the pseudonymous author whose Twelve Keys became one of the most influential alchemical texts, established the wolf as the agent of royal purification: the king's body must be cast to the wolf, consumed, and then the wolf must itself be burned, so that the king may rise again, stronger and immortal.

Maier deploys the wolf most dramatically in Emblem XXIV of the Atalanta Fugiens, whose motto reads: "The wolf devoured the king and after the wolf had been burnt, it returned the king to life." The engraved plate shows the voracious wolf seizing upon the body of a crowned king, while in the background a great fire awaits the wolf's own destruction. The epigram instructs the adept to catch the voracious wolf, throw the king's body before it, then cast the sated wolf onto the pyre, repeating the process until the king rises with a lion's heart. The discourse elaborates that the wolf roams through mountains and valleys seeking prey, and must come from cold regions where hunger makes wolves more savage, while the king travels exhausted from the East. In Emblem XLVII, the wolf appears paired with a dog: the wolf comes from the East (sulphur, hot and choleric) and the dog from the West (mercury, soft and mouldable), and they bite each other in furious combat until both perish, their mutual destruction producing the unified Stone.

De Jong traces Emblem XXIV directly to the first of Basil Valentine's Twelve Keys, published in the Tripus Aureus that Maier himself edited. She identifies the king as the symbol of the Philosophers' Stone in its impure state, which must be destroyed by the wolf (antimony) and purified by fire, after which the king rises immortal as the tinctura that cures all diseases. De Jong notes that the wolf is subject to the bellicose Mars by name but is a son of grey-haired Saturn by birth, linking antimony to both the martial and saturnine registers of alchemical symbolism. For Emblem XLVII, De Jong traces the wolf-and-dog pairing to the Consilium Conjugii and Rhazes' letter, explaining that East gives the grim wolf (sulphur) and West gives the dog (mercury), their mutual combat representing the first violent union of the two principles.

The wolf emblem participates in Maier's characteristic pedagogy of visual puzzle-solving. The reader must decipher why destruction leads to resurrection, why the most voracious and lowly creature becomes the instrument of royal renewal. The paradox is chemical: antimony, a base and corrosive substance, purifies gold by consuming its impurities in fire. But the paradox is also spiritual: the king must descend into humiliation, be devoured by the lowest of animals, and pass through fire before he can reign. Maier's wolf invites the reader to see purification not as gentle refinement but as violent consumption and sacrificial death, a theme that resonates across the nigredo emblems of the Atalanta Fugiens.
