Apocalyptic Woman / Turkish Madonna FIGURE

Mulier Apocalyptica
The Woman of Revelation 12 — identified by Szulakowska as the Virgin Mary in Maier's alchemical Eucharist, connected to Ottoman war imagery.

The Apocalyptic Woman is the figure described in Revelation 12:1 as 'a woman clothed with the sun, with the moon under her feet, and on her head a crown of twelve stars.' This biblical image, laden with cosmological and Mariological symbolism, was adopted by early modern alchemical writers as a cipher for the conjunction of solar and lunar principles — the union of gold and silver, masculine and feminine, fixed and volatile — within a framework that invested chemical transformation with sacramental significance.

The figure appears not in the Atalanta Fugiens itself but in Maier's Symbola Aureae Mensae (1617), engraved by Matthaeus Merian in an illustration depicting an alchemical mass scene. Szulakowska identifies this image as one of the earliest examples of the 'Turkish Madonna' — a form of Marian iconography that emerged during the Ottoman wars as a symbol of Christendom's defense against Islamic expansion. In Maier's illustration, the Apocalyptic Woman presides over a scene that identifies the philosopher's stone with Christ and the alchemical process with the Eucharistic transformation of bread and wine.

Szulakowska argues that this illustration constitutes what she terms the 'alchemical Eucharist' — a symbolic usurpation of the Catholic rite of Communion by Paracelsian alchemists, including Khunrath, Maier, and Fludd, who identified their chemical procedures with the transubstantiation of the Host. The Woman's role as producer of lac virginis (virgin's milk) connects her to the alchemical tradition of volatile spirit as feminine nourishment, a motif that resonates with the nursing imagery throughout Maier's emblem sequences.

Within the Atalanta Fugiens, the Apocalyptic Woman's presence is indirect but detectable. Emblem XL, with its two fountains and its discourse on the elixir of life, contains what Szulakowska identifies as Maier's most overt reference to the alchemical sacrament: the elixir is described as analogous to 'the water of life of Christ, meaning by this both the sacrament of Baptism and also that of the Eucharist.' The nursing motif of Emblem V, where a woman suckles a toad, likewise connects to the lac virginis tradition that the Apocalyptic Woman embodies.

De Jong does not treat the Apocalyptic Woman as a separate figure in her analysis, focusing instead on the classical mythological parallels. Szulakowska's reading adds a distinctly Christian theological dimension to the AF's symbolic program, arguing that beneath the classical mythological surface lies a concealed Eucharistic theology. This interpretation remains contested: Forshaw, following Tilton, emphasizes that Maier understood his work as natural rather than supernatural, and that the sacramental overtones may reflect the broader cultural milieu rather than Maier's specific intentions.

For a general reader, the Apocalyptic Woman illustrates how alchemical writers folded biblical imagery into their symbolic vocabulary. A figure from the Book of Revelation becomes a vessel for encoding the union of solar and lunar forces, the production of the life-giving elixir, and the claim that chemical transformation participates in the same divine economy as the central rite of Christian worship.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The 'woman clothed with the sun, with the moon under her feet' (Revelation 12) repurposed by alchemy as the perfected matter pregnant with the Stone, encompassing solar and lunar principles in a single radiant figure.
Medical
The supreme medicine personified as celestial bride: the panacea whose conception and bringing-to-birth requires the conjunction of Sol and Luna within the Paracelsian vessel-as-womb.
Spiritual
The Sophianic feminine of Christian Hermetism; the wisdom-figure whose appearance signals the soul's apocalyptic transformation and the breaking-in of the new aeon.
Cosmological
The cosmos as a single luminous bride: sun-clothed, moon-footed, star-crowned—the mediating world-soul whose imminent delivery is the cosmic counterpart of the alchemical work.

In Atalanta Fugiens

While the Apocalyptic Woman appears in the Symbola Aureae Mensae rather than directly in the Atalanta Fugiens, Szulakowska argues that the same sacramental theology underlies both works. AF Emblem XL's two fountains are identified as Maier's most overt reference to the alchemical Eucharist — the elixir of life described as analogous to 'the water of life of Christ, meaning both Baptism and the Eucharist.' The nursing motif in AF Emblem V connects to the lac virginis tradition.
