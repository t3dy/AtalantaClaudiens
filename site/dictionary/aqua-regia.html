Aqua Regia SUBSTANCE

Royal water; the only solvent capable of dissolving gold, composed of nitric and hydrochloric acids. Symbol of the power to dissolve the noblest metal.

Aqua regia, literally "royal water" or "king's water," is the name alchemists gave to the only known solvent capable of dissolving gold, the noblest of metals. The mixture, composed of nitric acid and hydrochloric acid in approximately a one-to-three ratio, earned its regal title precisely because it could dissolve the "king" of metals, a feat no single acid could accomplish alone. The preparation and use of aqua regia was known to Arabic alchemists from at least the eighth century, and recipes for it appear in the works attributed to Jabir ibn Hayyan (Geber) and later in European compilations such as the Pseudo-Geberian Summa Perfectionis. For medieval and early modern practitioners, aqua regia represented a profound paradox: the most perfect substance in nature required the most violent agent of destruction before it could be reduced to its philosophical essence.

Within the broader framework of alchemical theory, aqua regia occupied an important conceptual position because it demonstrated that even gold was not truly indestructible. If the king of metals could be dissolved, then the fundamental assumption of transmutation theory held firm: all metals shared a common origin in prima materia and could, in principle, be reduced to that origin and reconstituted in perfected form. The ability to dissolve gold was therefore not merely a laboratory technique but a philosophical proof that nature's hierarchy was mutable. Alchemical writers frequently distinguished between common solvents, which could dissolve lesser metals, and the "royal" solvent required for gold, framing this distinction as a measure of the solvent's power and nobility.

In Atalanta Fugiens, Maier does not dedicate a single emblem exclusively to aqua regia, but the concept pervades his dissolution imagery. The broader theme of dissolving, purifying, and reconstituting matter runs through multiple emblems, particularly those dealing with the washing and cleansing operations. Emblem VI, which instructs the reader to "sow your gold in the white foliated earth," presupposes that gold has first been reduced to a sowing-capable state, an operation that in practical terms would require dissolution. Emblem XII, with its imagery of the Stone requiring extraction through fire and force, likewise gestures toward the violent dissolution that gold must undergo. Emblem XXVIII, featuring the allegory of King Duenech who must be washed and purified in a steam-bath, encodes a dissolution process in which the black bile (impurity) is driven away.

De Jong traces Maier's dissolution imagery to the Turba Philosophorum and the Rosarium Philosophorum tradition, where the destruction of gold is presented as a necessary precondition for its philosophical rebirth. She notes that the alchemical paradox of destroying perfection in order to achieve a higher perfection is central to Maier's entire emblematic program, running from the nigredo imagery of the early emblems through to the final reconstitution in Emblem L.

In chemical terms, aqua regia works because the combination of nitric and hydrochloric acids produces nascent chlorine and nitrosyl chloride, which together attack the gold surface and form soluble gold chloride (auric chloride, HAuCl4). This reaction was empirically known long before its chemistry was understood, and alchemists correctly recognized that neither acid alone could achieve the dissolution. The practical knowledge embedded in alchemical recipes for aqua regia represents genuine chemical expertise, even when wrapped in allegorical language about killing the king so that he might be reborn.

Scholars including Tilton and Pagel have emphasized that Maier's interest in dissolution was not purely theoretical. As a trained physician who practiced iatrochemistry, Maier understood the medical applications of chemically prepared remedies, and the dissolution of gold was directly relevant to the preparation of aurum potabile, the drinkable gold that was central to his medical philosophy.

For the lay reader encountering this term, aqua regia is best understood as the alchemists' most powerful tool of transformation. Just as a sculptor must break stone to reveal a statue, the alchemist had to destroy gold's physical perfection to access its philosophical essence. The term survives in modern chemistry, where aqua regia is still used in laboratories to dissolve noble metals and in the refining of gold.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The royal water: a mixture of nitric and hydrochloric acids capable of dissolving gold—the only solvent powerful enough to attack the noblest metal.
Medical
A corrosive remedy of extreme potency, used in minute doses to dissolve calcified obstructions that milder treatments cannot address.
Spiritual
The supreme solvent of ego: a crisis or ordeal so powerful it dissolves even the most fixed and defended aspects of the personality.
Cosmological
The primordial deluge or universal solvent; the catastrophic dissolution that reduces even the most permanent cosmic structures to their elements.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Maier references the concept of a universal solvent capable of dissolving even gold within the broader dissolution imagery of Atalanta Fugiens. De Jong connects this to the alchemical paradox that the most noble substance requires the most powerful agent of destruction before it can be reconstituted in perfected form.
