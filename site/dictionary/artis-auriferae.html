Artis Auriferae Quam Chemiam Vocant

The Artis Auriferae, Quam Chemiam Vocant ("Of the Gold-Making Art, Which They Call Chemistry") is one of the most important alchemical anthologies ever assembled. First published in Basel in 1572 by Petrum Pernam in two volumes, it gathers dozens of foundational alchemical treatises into a single compendium. The collection includes two versions of the Turba Philosophorum, the Visio Arislei and its enigmas, the Aurora Consurgens, the Rosarium Philosophorum with its celebrated woodcuts, the Scala Philosophorum, the Clangor Buccinae, and writings attributed to Geber, Maria Prophetissa, Avicenna, Roger Bacon, and many others. A second edition appeared in 1593 under Conrad Waldkirch, richly decorated, and a three-volume edition followed in 1610.

As a compendium, the Artis Auriferae served as a library in miniature for the early modern alchemist. Its first volume alone contains twenty-two distinct treatises spanning cosmological speculation, practical laboratory instruction, and allegorical narrative. The second volume opens with the Liber de Compositione Alchemiae of Morienus Romanus and proceeds through the Scala Philosophorum, the Rosarium Philosophorum, and the writings of Arnaldus de Villanova and Roger Bacon. The breadth of the collection meant that an alchemist who owned the Artis Auriferae had access to the core textual tradition of Latin alchemy from its Arabic roots through to the late medieval compilations.

Michael Maier drew upon the Artis Auriferae as a primary sourcebook for his Atalanta Fugiens. Many of the mottos and discourse arguments in Maier's emblems derive from treatises contained in this anthology. The motto for Emblem I, drawn from the Tabula Smaragdina, reaches Maier through the Turba tradition preserved here. The Visio Arislei in Volume I supplies the allegory of Thabritius and Beya that informs Emblems XX and XXV. The Rosarium Philosophorum in Volume II provides Geber's pronouncements on the medicinal power of the Elixir that appear in the discourse of the later emblems. Maier's citations frequently reference page numbers in the Artis Auriferae directly, confirming that he worked with the physical volumes at hand.

De Jong identifies the Artis Auriferae as one of the essential collective works through which Maier accessed the medieval alchemical tradition (De Jong, bibliography, pp. 273-277). She traces specific mottos and discourse passages in Emblems I, III, V, VIII, XIV, and XXI back to treatises within the anthology, demonstrating that Maier did not encounter these texts in isolation but as part of the curated assemblage the Artis Auriferae provided. De Jong notes that the anthology's organization shaped Maier's understanding of the relationships between texts: the Turba, the Visio Arislei, and the Allegoriae appear sequentially in Volume I, presenting what amounts to a narrative arc from cosmological debate through allegorical vision to practical instruction. Maier also discusses the Artis Auriferae in his Symbola Aureae Mensae (pp. 273-277), confirming his direct engagement with the collection.

For the modern reader, the Artis Auriferae is best understood as the alchemist's equivalent of a scriptural canon. Just as medieval theologians worked from compiled volumes of patristic commentary, alchemists like Maier inherited their tradition through anthologies that placed Arabic, pseudo-Greek, and Latin texts side by side. The Artis Auriferae was not merely a reference work but a site of intellectual formation: the juxtapositions it created between texts became part of how alchemists read and thought. When Maier constructed the Atalanta Fugiens as a multimedia emblem book weaving together image, music, and text, he was building upon the anthological tradition that the Artis Auriferae exemplified, transforming a library of sources into a unified work of art.
