Atalanta FIGURE

The fleeing maiden of the frontispiece; represents volatile Mercury that must be captured by the fixed principle.

Atalanta is the swift-footed huntress of Greek mythology, celebrated in two distinct mythological traditions: the Arcadian Atalanta who participated in the Calydonian boar hunt, and the Boeotian Atalanta whose footrace with Hippomenes is told in Ovid's Metamorphoses, Book X. It is the Ovidian version that Maier adopts as the governing allegory of his entire work. In Ovid's telling, Atalanta's father, warned by an oracle that marriage would be her undoing, decreed that she would only marry the man who could outrun her — and that all who tried and failed would be put to death. Hippomenes, aided by Venus's gift of three golden apples from the Garden of the Hesperides, won the race by throwing the apples to distract Atalanta from her course.

Maier makes Atalanta the titular figure of the Atalanta Fugiens — literally 'Atalanta Fleeing' — and she appears prominently on the frontispiece, depicted racing against Hippomenes at the bottom of the title-page composition. Venus stands to the right, handing the golden apples to Hippomenes. The entire frontispiece, as De Jong demonstrates, presents the alchemical process in miniature: the golden apples descend from the Garden of the Hesperides (the divine source), Hippomenes pursues Atalanta (Sulphur pursues Mercury), they unite in Cybele's temple, and are transformed into lions.

In Maier's alchemical reading, Atalanta represents the volatile mercurial principle — philosophical Mercury in its fugitive, ungraspable aspect. She is the substance that perpetually escapes the alchemist's grasp, that cannot be fixed by force alone. De Jong identifies Atalanta's flight as the structural logic governing all fifty emblems: each dramatizes some aspect of the pursuit between the fixed and the volatile, between Sulphur's desire for union and Mercury's resistance to capture (De Jong, pp. 314-316). The three-voice fugues that accompany each emblem reinforce this allegory musically, with one voice representing Atalanta's flight, another Hippomenes's pursuit, and a third the golden apple that mediates between them.

The frontispiece narrative does not end happily. After their union in Cybele's temple, the couple is punished for profaning the goddess's sanctuary by being transformed into lions. De Jong reads this as Maier's acknowledgment that the conjunction of Mercury and Sulphur is not the final step — the union must pass through a further transformation (the leonine phase represented by the winged and wingless lions of Emblem XVI) before the Work is complete. Atalanta's identity shifts from maiden to lioness, from volatile Mercury to the fixed, powerful substance that draws Cybele's chariot.

In Emblem XLIV's comprehensive catalogue of mythological equivalences, Maier lists Atalanta among the lunar-feminine figures alongside Isis, Juno, Venus, Latona, and others — all of whom represent aspects of the same receptive, volatile, mercurial principle. Robertus Vallensis's De Veritate et Antiquitate Artis Chemicae, which De Jong identifies as a direct source for Maier's frontispiece commentary, had already established the Atalanta-Hippomenes race as an allegory for the pursuit of the volatile by the fixed.

For a lay reader, Atalanta embodies the central paradox of the alchemical art: the substance the alchemist most desires is the one that constantly flees. The entire book is named for her flight, and the alchemist's task is not to overpower her but to use cunning, patience, and the right catalytic agents — the golden apples of knowledge — to bring the volatile spirit to rest.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Maier makes Atalanta the titular figure of his entire work, drawing on Ovid's Metamorphoses X to allegorize the volatile mercurial principle as a swift maiden who can only be overtaken through cunning rather than force. De Jong demonstrates that Atalanta's flight structures the allegorical logic of all fifty emblems, each of which dramatizes some aspect of the pursuit between volatile and fixed.
