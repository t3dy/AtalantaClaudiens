Aurum Potabile SUBSTANCE

Drinkable gold — the supreme alchemical medicine; Maier devoted his De Circulo Physico Quadrato (1616) to this concept.

Aurum potabile, drinkable gold, represents one of the most significant intersections between alchemical theory and medical practice in the early modern period. The concept rests on the ancient belief that gold, as the most perfect and incorruptible of metals, must contain healing properties proportional to its perfection. If gold resisted all natural agents of corruption, then a medicine derived from gold should, in principle, resist disease and decay within the human body. The challenge was to render gold, which is chemically inert and insoluble in ordinary solvents, into a form that the body could absorb. This problem occupied alchemical physicians from at least the thirteenth century, when writers such as Pseudo-Arnald of Villanova and Roger Bacon described elixirs based on gold preparations.

The preparation of aurum potabile typically involved dissolving gold in aqua regia or other strong solvents, then subjecting the solution to repeated distillation and purification until a drinkable tincture was produced. The resulting preparations varied enormously in composition and safety, from relatively harmless colloidal gold suspensions to extremely dangerous mercury and antimony compounds that happened to contain trace amounts of gold. De Jong notes that Chinese alchemy, with its strong emphasis on the medicine of immortality and the elixir of long life, developed similar concepts independently, and that Arabic alchemy paid considerable attention to aurum potabile alongside the elixir of life, suggesting cross-cultural transmission of the idea.

Maier devoted an entire treatise to this concept: De Circulo Physico Quadrato (1616), in which he argues that gold occupies a central position among metals analogous to the Sun's position among the planets and the heart's position in the human body. Between these three centers, Maier proposes, there exists a circular connection by which God gives strength to the Sun, the Sun ripens metals in the earth into gold, gold strengthens the human heart, and through the strengthened heart, the living soul turns toward God. Aurum potabile is the medium through which this cosmic circuit becomes therapeutically effective.

The most dramatic historical episode connected to aurum potabile in Maier's biography involves his relationship with Francis Anthony, an English physician whom Tilton discusses at length. Anthony, a Cambridge graduate and son of a London goldsmith, became famous and controversial for peddling his alchemical remedy, the aurum potabile, which was described as a powerful cathartic and emetic. Tilton documents that Anthony's preparation was said to have miraculously cured certain patients but also seriously injured or killed others. Anthony was imprisoned by the College of Physicians for practicing without a license, but Maier publicly defended him, writing a commendatory letter in Anthony's Medicinae Chymicae et Veri Potabilis Auri Assertio (1616). Maier's defense of Anthony reveals his conviction that gold-based medicines, properly prepared, represented the highest achievement of medical alchemy.

Maier distinguished his own preparation from Anthony's, however, stating that his medicine was similar to aurum potabile except that it was extracted not from elemental gold but from philosophical gold, which he cryptically described as conceivable only in the imagination. This distinction suggests that Maier's aurum potabile operated on a level that transcended simple metallurgy: the drinkable gold was not merely a chemical preparation but a vehicle for philosophical and spiritual virtue.

In Atalanta Fugiens, the concept of drinkable gold surfaces indirectly in the medical imagery of Emblem XLVIII, where the sick King Merlinus is treated by physicians administering curative potions, and in Emblem IX, which deals with the tree of philosophy and the solar force that ripens all things. The entire medicinal dimension of the emblem sequence reflects Maier's conviction that the Philosopher's Stone and aurum potabile were ultimately the same thing viewed from different angles: the Stone transmutes metals, while the drinkable gold heals bodies.

Craven notes that the concept of aurum potabile was invoked in contexts reaching far beyond the laboratory. General Hitchcock is quoted declaring that the Stone is the true aurum potabile, the true quintessence, identifying drinkable gold with divine love itself. This spiritual reading, while not Maier's explicit position, reflects the broader Hermetic tradition in which physical gold, philosophical gold, and spiritual illumination existed on a single continuum.

In modern chemistry, colloidal gold nanoparticles are indeed used in medical diagnostics and certain therapeutic applications, lending a retroactive plausibility to the alchemists' intuition that gold in extremely fine division could interact with biological systems. The historical preparations, however, were often toxic due to the mercury, antimony, and strong acids involved in their production.

For the lay reader, aurum potabile represents the alchemists' boldest medical claim: that the perfection of gold could be unlocked and administered as a universal medicine. It stands at the crossroads of metallurgy, medicine, and mysticism, a place where Maier spent much of his intellectual life.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Aurum potabile represents the practical medical goal underlying Maier's alchemical project. Godwin reveals that Maier claimed to have produced the Universal Medicine during his Kiel laboratory period (1602-1608), describing it as 'of a bright lemon color.' The AF's emphasis on the philosopher's stone as both a transmuting agent and a healing elixir reflects this dual aspiration — material transformation and medical cure as aspects of the same alchemical operation.
