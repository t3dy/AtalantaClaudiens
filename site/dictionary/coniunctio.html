Coniunctio PROCESS

Coniunctio Oppositorum
Union of opposites (male/female, volatile/fixed, Sun/Moon). The central operation of the Great Work.

Coniunctio, the conjunction or union of opposites, is the central operation and governing idea of the alchemical opus. It denotes the bringing together of two contrary principles — male and female, sulphur and mercury, sun and moon, fixed and volatile, king and queen — into a single, perfected unity. The entire trajectory of the Great Work can be understood as a preparation for this union: the preliminary operations of dissolution, purification, and calcination exist to separate, refine, and prepare the contraries so that they can be joined in a manner that produces something qualitatively new. The coniunctio is not merely a mixing or blending but a genuine synthesis in which the opposing principles are simultaneously destroyed and reborn as a higher unity.

The concept draws on deep philosophical roots. Empedocles of Akragas posited love and strife as the two cosmic forces that alternately unite and separate the elements. The Hermetic tradition, transmitted through the Tabula Smaragdina and the Turba Philosophorum, frames the alchemical work as the reconciliation of contraries through a mediating substance. Carl Gustav Jung devoted his final major work, Mysterium Coniunctionis, to demonstrating that the alchemists' conjunction of opposites anticipated his own psychology of individuation. Tilton identifies the coniunctio oppositorum as the central idea of alchemical procedure in Jung's reading, noting that Jung considered Maier's work particularly rich in this symbolism.

In Atalanta Fugiens, coniunctio is the most pervasive theme, appearing across at least fourteen emblems according to De Jong's analysis. The most explicit depiction is Emblem IV, where the motto instructs the reader to join brother and sister and give them the cup of love. De Jong identifies the brother and sister as symbols of the elemental contrasts whose opposition must be converted into unity through the love potion. The alchemical theory underlying this emblem derives from Empedocles' conception of the attracting and repelling forces between elements, with love as the cosmic principle that draws contraries into union. The love potion represents the dissolving power by which the contraries are disintegrated into their primary matter and then recombined.

Emblem XXXIII presents the coniunctio in its achieved form through the hermaphrodite, the figure that combines male and female in a single body. The hermaphrodite represents the union of fixed and volatile, sun and moon, accomplished through the complete cycle of dissolution and reconstitution. De Jong connects this figure to the Rosarium Philosophorum's woodcut sequence, where the conjoined king and queen produce a hermaphroditic child — the philosophical Stone. The hermaphrodite stands at the climax of the conjunction sequence, embodying the perfected unity of all contraries.

The frontispiece of the Atalanta Fugiens dramatizes the coniunctio as a race. Atalanta, the fleeing maiden, represents volatile mercury; Hippomenes, the pursuing youth, represents fixed sulphur; and the golden apples represent the catalytic agent that enables their union. De Jong demonstrates that this pursuit-and-capture narrative structures the allegorical logic of all fifty emblems. The Ovidian myth of the race culminates in the lovers' embrace in Cybele's temple and their subsequent transformation into lions — a transformation that, in Maier's alchemical reading, signifies the fixation of the volatile into a powerful, stable form.

The theme recurs through multiple mythological and allegorical variants: the union of Sol and Luna in Emblem XXXIV, the marriage that produces the hermaphrodite, the mutual destruction of dragon and woman in Emblem L. De Jong and Pagel both emphasize that Maier understands the conjunction not as a single event but as a process that must be achieved repeatedly at different levels — chemical, cosmological, and spiritual. Pagel describes the entire Atalanta Fugiens as structured around the principles of the union of opposites, with sulphur and mercury as the principal components of this conjunction.

For the non-specialist, coniunctio is the marriage at the heart of alchemy — the moment when two opposed forces that have been carefully separated and purified are brought together to produce something neither could have become alone. It is the alchemical version of the insight that creation emerges from the meeting of differences: day and night, heat and cold, form and matter. Every wedding image, every embrace, every battle that ends in mutual absorption points to this central mystery of the alchemical tradition.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The chemical wedding of Sulphur and Mercury—the union of the red king and white queen in the sealed vessel to produce the hermaphroditic Rebis.
Medical
The rebalancing of contrary humors into harmonious proportion; the therapeutic union of hot and cold, moist and dry principles within the body.
Spiritual
The sacred marriage of opposites within the psyche—conscious and unconscious, masculine and feminine—producing wholeness and inner unity.
Cosmological
The conjunction of Sol and Luna, the great celestial marriage whose earthly reflection governs generation and the harmony of macrocosm and microcosm.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Maier presents the union of opposites as the central mystery of the Work, depicting it through the marriage of brother and sister (Emblem IV) and the hermaphrodite figure (Emblem XXXIII). De Jong traces this theme across 14 emblems, identifying it as the structural backbone of the entire emblem sequence.
