Cybele FIGURE

Phrygian mother goddess who transforms Atalanta and Hippomenes into lions as punishment for profaning her temple.

Cybele (also Kybele) is the Phrygian mother goddess adopted into Greek and Roman religion as Magna Mater, the Great Mother. In the myth of Atalanta and Hippomenes retold by Ovid and adopted by Maier, the young couple consummate their love in Cybele's temple, provoking the goddess to transform them into the lions that pull her chariot. In Maier's alchemical reading, this transformation represents the fixation of the volatile (Atalanta/Mercury) and the fixed (Hippomenes/Sulphur) into a stable, powerful form — the lions being traditional alchemical symbols for mature, activated substances.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Cybele appears on the AF frontispiece where the transformation of Atalanta and Hippomenes into lions is depicted. The couple embraces in her temple entrance before emerging behind it as lion and lioness. Forshaw notes that Cybele's chariot is traditionally pulled by lions, connecting the mythological punishment to the alchemical symbolism of the winged and wingless lions that recur throughout the emblem series.
