Dissolution PROCESS

Dissolutio
Breaking down solid form into liquid; releasing the fixed into the volatile.

Dissolution is the alchemical operation of breaking down a solid substance into a liquid state, returning differentiated, structured matter to a fluid, undifferentiated condition. It is one of the two fundamental movements of the alchemical opus — the other being coagulation — and is typically associated with the early stages of the Work, when the existing form of the prima materia must be destroyed before it can be reconstituted in perfected form. The alchemists employed a range of practical solvents and corrosive agents to achieve dissolution, but the operation carried a symbolic weight far exceeding its laboratory application: dissolution meant the death of the old form, the release of the hidden spirit, and the return to the formless potentiality from which new creation could emerge.

The Turba Philosophorum, one of the foundational texts of the Latin alchemical tradition, teaches that all creation begins in liquefaction — a principle that De Jong identifies as governing Maier's treatment of dissolution throughout the Atalanta Fugiens. The alchemists' axiom solve et coagula places dissolution first: the fixed must be dissolved before the volatile can be coagulated. The dissolution of the Stone (dissolutio lapidis) represents the necessary destruction of composed matter in order to discover the primary matter from which the Philosophers' Stone can evolve. De Jong identifies this operation as the first stage of the four-stage preparation described in the source for Emblem V's motto.

In Atalanta Fugiens, dissolution appears in multiple dramatic guises. Emblem IV depicts the joining of brother and sister through a love potion that contains a dissolving power, by which the contraries are disintegrated into extremely small pieces. De Jong connects this disintegration to the atomistic theory of Leucippus and Democritus, suggesting that Maier understood the dissolution as a splitting of combined atoms into their smallest, indivisible particles. Emblem V depicts the reciprocal dissolution-coagulation dynamic: as the toad coagulates and grows, the woman dissolves and dies. The Latin axiom that De Jong identifies as the key to this emblem states that every solution is a mortification — dissolution and death are the same process.

The imagery of dissolution pervades the middle and late emblems. Emblem VII shows a young bird that attempts to fly from its nest but falls back, representing the volatile mercury that must be sublimated so often that it becomes solid. The discourse of Emblem VII explains the necessity of dissolution: the body becomes white through dissolution, while the spirit becomes red. Emblem XLIV depicts the death of Osiris, whose body is disintegrated into minutissimas partes — the most minute parts — as a figure for the complete dissolution of matter that precedes its reconstitution. Emblem L, the final emblem, presents the mutual dissolution of the woman and the serpent in each other's blood, the culminating dissolution that completes the cycle.

De Jong emphasizes that Maier understands dissolution as both destructive and generative. The killing of the elements in Emblem XIX, the drowning of the Chemical King in Emblem XXXI, the dissolution of the Merlini-King in the waters of Emblem XLVIII — all these deaths serve the larger pattern of death and resurrection that structures the entire opus. The Rosarium Philosophorum's climactic image of Christ rising from the sarcophagus represents the philosophical Stone as the product of dissolution-followed-by-resurrection, the same pattern that Maier enacts through his mythological and allegorical variants.

For the lay reader, dissolution is the alchemical recognition that nothing new can be created without first destroying what already exists. A seed must rot in the earth before a plant can grow; a caterpillar must dissolve within its chrysalis before a butterfly can emerge. The alchemists raised this natural observation to the level of a universal principle: all transformation begins with the breaking apart of the old form, and the willingness to endure this breaking is the first requirement of the Work.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
Dissolving the calcined matter in the mercurial water; the body is liquefied so that its hidden seed may be released from the fixed matrix.
Medical
The therapeutic dissolving of calcified obstructions and hardened humoral deposits; restoring fluidity to the body's circulatory economy.
Spiritual
The dissolution of rigid psychic structures; the letting go of fixed identities and beliefs that obstruct the flow of inner transformation.
Cosmological
Water's primordial action on earth; the lunar solvent that returns differentiated forms to the undifferentiated ocean of potentiality.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Maier frames dissolution as both destructive and generative, from the woman's death in Emblem V to the mutual annihilation of dragon and woman in Emblem L. De Jong identifies the final emblem's return to primary matter as the culminating dissolution that completes the Work, drawing on the Turba's teaching that all creation begins in liquefaction.
