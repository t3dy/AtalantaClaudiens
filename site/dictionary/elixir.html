Elixir SUBSTANCE

The perfected medicine capable of healing all disease and transmuting metals; synonymous with the Philosopher's Stone in its medicinal aspect.

The Elixir, from the Arabic al-iksir (itself possibly derived from the Greek xerion, a dry powder for treating wounds), denotes the perfected product of the alchemical art in its capacity as universal medicine and transmuting agent. The term entered Western European alchemical vocabulary through Latin translations of Arabic texts, particularly those attributed to Jabir ibn Hayyan and the Jabir corpus, where the concept of using the elixir as a transmutation agent to "heal" imperfect metals represents, as De Jong notes, the beginning of iatrochemistry. In Arabic alchemy, the elixir and the Philosopher's Stone were often treated as synonymous, both designating the substance capable of perfecting whatever it touched.

In medieval and early modern European alchemy, the Elixir developed distinct medical and metallurgical dimensions. As a metallurgical agent, it was the tinctura rubea, the red tincture capable of transmuting base metals into gold. As a medical agent, it was the universal medicine or panacea capable of curing all diseases and extending life indefinitely. De Jong demonstrates that the Tabula Smaragdina commentaries all agreed that the forces of the cosmic soul had to be concentrated into one solid matter called the Philosopher's Stone, or the Elixir, or the Tinctura Rubea, by which everything imperfect, ignoble, or ill could be brought to perfection. This triple identification of Stone, Elixir, and Tincture as different names for the same substance was standard in the tradition Maier inherited.

The medical dimension of the Elixir was particularly important to Maier, who was himself a trained physician. De Jong notes that Chinese alchemy emphasized the preparation of the medicine of immortality and the elixir of long life, and that Arabic alchemy similarly devoted extensive attention to the elixir of life and aurum potabile. This cross-cultural convergence on the idea of an alchemical medicine of extraordinary potency formed part of the intellectual background against which Maier composed Atalanta Fugiens.

In Atalanta Fugiens, the Elixir appears most directly in Emblem XLVIII, the allegory of the sick King Merlinus who is treated by successive physicians from Egypt, Alexandria, and elsewhere, each administering different remedies until the king's health is restored and "both his cheeks were coloured rose-red." De Jong traces this allegory to the Merlini Allegoria in the Artis Auriferae and reads the sick king as the imperfect matter of the opus that must be "healed" through the application of increasingly refined medicines until it achieves the red color signifying perfection. The physicians who attend the king represent successive stages of purification, and the final cure is the achievement of the Elixir itself: the perfected red substance that restores health to the sick metal-king.

De Jong further connects the Elixir to the broader Christological symbolism in Maier's work. She cites passages equating the Philosopher's Stone with Christ, noting that just as the Stone or Elixir purifies imperfect metals and transmutes them into gold, so Christ with his rose-colored blood purges sinners and perfects imperfect humanity. This theological dimension does not replace the chemical meaning but layers upon it, creating the characteristic multi-register symbolism of Maier's emblematic program.

Pagel, writing from the perspective of the history of medicine, emphasizes that the Elixir concept bridges alchemical transmutation and therapeutic practice, and that Maier's work represents a significant moment in the development of iatrochemistry, where the preparation of chemical medicines began to rival and eventually displace the vegetable remedies of traditional Galenic medicine.

In chemical terms, the various substances historically identified as the Elixir ranged from sophisticated preparations of antimony, mercury, and gold compounds to simpler mineral tinctures. The red color consistently associated with the perfected Elixir may reflect empirical observation of red precipitates formed in certain metallic reactions, such as red mercuric oxide or colloidal gold preparations.

For the museum visitor, the Elixir is the alchemists' supreme aspiration: a single substance that could heal all disease in the body and all imperfection in metals, bridging the gap between medicine and metallurgy through the conviction that the same cosmic principles governed both. It is the answer to the question that drives the entire Atalanta Fugiens: what is the secret of nature that, once grasped, transforms everything it touches?

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The perfected tincture in liquid form: identical in substance to the Philosopher's Stone but prepared as a drinkable solution for medicinal projection.
Medical
The universal medicine capable of curing all diseases, restoring youth, and prolonging life indefinitely—the supreme goal of iatrochemistry.
Spiritual
The draught of immortality; the concentrated essence of spiritual realization that, once imbibed, permanently transforms the aspirant's nature.
Cosmological
The quintessence or fifth element: the celestial substance free from corruption that permeates and sustains the four mundane elements.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Maier's depiction of the curative potion administered to King Duenech in Emblem XLVIII encodes the Elixir as the perfected alchemical medicine. De Jong and Pagel both emphasize the medical dimension of AF where the Elixir bridges alchemical transmutation and therapeutic practice.
