Fugue MUSICAL

Fuga
Three-voice musical canon accompanying each emblem. Voices represent Atalanta (fleeing/volatile), Hippomenes (pursuing/fixed), and Golden Apple (catalyst).

The fugue (Latin fuga, meaning flight) is a musical form in which a theme introduced by one voice is successively taken up by additional voices in overlapping entries, creating a texture of imitation, pursuit, and eventual resolution. In the context of Atalanta Fugiens, the term refers specifically to the fifty three-voice canons that Michael Maier composed to accompany each emblem — an unprecedented integration of music into the alchemical emblem book genre that makes Maier's work unique in the history of both alchemy and music. The title of the work itself is a double entendre: Atalanta Fugiens means both the fleeing Atalanta (from the Ovidian myth) and the Atalanta of the fugues, binding the musical and mythological dimensions into a single conceptual unity.

Maier explains his musical conception in the preface to the reader. Just as Atalanta flees, one musical voice always flees in front of the other, and the other follows like Hippomenes, while they are stabilized and grounded by the third voice, which is simple and of constant rhythm, as if by a golden apple. The three voices of each fugue thus allegorize the three participants in the mythological race: the vox fugiens (fleeing voice) represents Atalanta, the volatile mercurial principle; the vox sequens (pursuing voice) represents Hippomenes, the fixed sulphurous principle; and the cantus firmus (the constant, grounding voice) represents the golden apple, the catalytic agent that mediates between the contraries and enables their union.

The cantus firmus that underpins all fifty fugues was identified by Helen Joy Sleeper in 1938 as the Christe eleison from Gregorian Mass IV, Cunctipotens genitor. This liturgical melody, drawn from the most sacred text of the Christian Mass, gives the fugues a devotional and cosmological dimension that extends far beyond their alchemical allegory. In pre-Reformation practice, the Christe eleison was sung three times during the Mass, and Maier structures each fuga so that the epigram couplets are sung three times — a deliberate mirroring of liturgical form. The choice of a sacred cantus firmus connects the alchemical work to the Eucharistic theology that Szulakowska identifies as underlying Maier's entire project.

Wescott's musicological analysis demonstrates that Maier employs the Renaissance system of planetary modes to encode alchemical information within the musical structure itself. Each mode was associated with a planet, a metal, and a set of affective qualities. Saturn corresponds to the Mixolydian mode and lead; the Sun corresponds to the Dorian mode and gold; Venus corresponds to the Hypolydian mode and copper. Wescott shows that Maier manipulates the modal character of individual fugues to express the alchemical stage depicted in the accompanying emblem. The King emblems (XXIV, XXVIII, XXXI, XLIV), which depict Saturnian Putrefaction, employ harsh dissonances and the darker modes, while emblems depicting Albification employ smoother, more consonant settings in modes associated with Venus.

The fugues operate within the tradition of musica speculativa — the medieval and Renaissance understanding of music as a mathematical-philosophical discipline revealing the harmonic structure of the cosmos. Maier's subtitle promises content to be looked at, read, meditated, understood, weighed, sung, and listened to — a comprehensive engagement of all the senses and faculties. De Jong notes the strong relation between alchemy and music that had already been expressed in the writings of Zosimos and Stephanos, connected with Pythagorean views about the music of the spheres. Maier sees music in the sense of the medieval quadrivium, where musica, beside arithmetica, geometria, and astronomia, gives admittance to philosophia and theologia.

Hasler and Leedy have both argued that the fugues require performative engagement — they are not merely decorative but functional, designed to be sung aloud as part of a meditative practice that attunes the singer's soul to the cosmic harmonies governing alchemical transformation. The act of singing the fugue, with its three voices pursuing and fleeing and resolving, enacts in sound the same drama of volatile and fixed that the emblem depicts in image and the discourse analyzes in text. The Atalanta Fugiens is thus an early experiment in multimedia — a work that demands simultaneous engagement through eye, ear, and intellect.

For the general reader, the fugues of the Atalanta Fugiens are best understood as the musical dimension of an alchemical teaching machine. Each emblem teaches through five channels simultaneously: the motto states the principle; the plate illustrates it visually; the epigram phrases it in verse; the discourse analyzes it in prose; and the fugue embodies it in sound. The three voices of the fugue dramatize the central alchemical drama — the chase between volatile and fixed, mediated by the catalytic agent — in a form that engages the body (through the physical act of singing) as well as the mind. No other alchemical work, before or since, has attempted this synthesis.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The contrapuntal form of Maier's fifty musical settings: three voices (Atalanta fleeing, Hippomenes pursuing, golden apple intervening) enact in sound the alchemical chase the emblems narrate visually.
Medical
The Ficinian therapy of polyphonic music: the sustained imitative voices treat the listener's spirit by entraining its rhythms into healthful proportion across multiple registers simultaneously.
Spiritual
The disciplined imitation of a single subject across difference; the pattern of contemplative practice in which the same insight returns transposed through varied life-circumstances until fully integrated.
Cosmological
The three voices as Sun, Moon, and the rotating Earth between them; the cosmos as great fugue in which celestial motions follow imitative rules across distinct registers.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Maier assigns each of the fifty emblems a three-voice fugue in which the voices allegorically represent Atalanta (the fleeing volatile principle), Hippomenes (the pursuing fixed principle), and the golden apple (the catalytic agent that enables their union). This unprecedented synthesis of music, image, and text makes Atalanta Fugiens unique in the history of emblem literature, as both Wescott and Leedy have demonstrated.
