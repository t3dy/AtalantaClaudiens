Geryon FIGURE

Three-bodied king of Spain whose cattle Hercules stole as his tenth labor; in AF, a figure for the composite nature of the prima materia.

Geryon is the three-bodied (or three-headed) giant king of Greek mythology who ruled the island of Erytheia in the far west, beyond the Pillars of Hercules. His prized red cattle were guarded by the herdsman Eurytion and the two-headed dog Orthrus. Hercules killed Geryon as his tenth labor in order to steal the cattle — a myth that, in the ancient world, was associated with the westward journey toward the setting sun and the land of the dead.

Maier invokes Geryon in the discourse of Emblem XIX, whose motto reads: 'If you kill one of the four, everybody will be dead immediately.' The epigram presents four brothers standing in a row, each holding one of the four elements (earth, water, air, fire), with the warning that killing any one brother destroys all four. In the discourse, Maier recounts the myth of Geryon's three bodies, his descent from Chrysaor and Medusa, and the seven-headed dragon that descended from Typhon and Echidna. Maier declares that taken literally this does not agree with historical truth, but that the story is rightly used in alchemical allegories.

De Jong traces Maier's reading of Geryon to the Turba Philosophorum's teaching on the inseparability of the Stone's components (De Jong, pp. 158-159). By Geryon having three bodies, with Hermes in mind, the three faces of one father should be understood — connecting to Emblem XLIX's motto about the Philosophical Child acknowledging three fathers. Others interpret the three as four, relating to the four elements. The emblem's central teaching is that the elements of the prima materia exist in such intimate union that isolating one destroys the work entirely. The analogy with twins united from birth reinforces the point: if one suffers, the other also suffers, and if one dies, the other also dies.

Alchemically, Geryon's three (or four) bodies represent the composite nature of the philosophical substance — whether understood as the tria prima (Mercury, Sulphur, Salt) or the four elements (earth, water, air, fire). Maier connects the emblem to the geometric formula of Emblem XXI, where the square (four elements) must be reduced to a triangle and then to a circle. The warning against killing any one brother is a practical instruction to the alchemist: do not attempt to isolate individual components through violent separation. The substance must be worked as a whole, its elements gently distinguished through graduated heat rather than forcibly torn apart.

Szulakowska adds a Christological reading, identifying the four-warriors motif as a disguised resurrection allegory in which the death and reconstitution of the composite body parallels Christ's death and resurrection. This interpretation aligns with her broader argument that Maier's classical mythological surface conceals a sacramental theology.

For a general reader, Geryon teaches the fundamental alchemical principle of holism: the philosopher's stone is not assembled from separate ingredients but coaxed from a single substance whose components are so intimately intertwined that they cannot be separated without destroying the whole. The three-bodied king is not a monster to be dismembered but a unity to be understood.
