Golden Apple CONCEPT

Pomum Aureum

The golden apple is the mythological object that gives the Atalanta Fugiens both its narrative framework and its deepest structural metaphor. In Ovid's Metamorphoses, the swift huntress Atalanta agrees to marry only the man who can outrun her; all who fail will die. Hippomenes defeats her not through superior speed but through cunning: during the race, he throws three golden apples given to him by Aphrodite, and Atalanta, unable to resist their beauty, stops to pick them up, losing the race. Maier seizes this myth as the governing allegory for his entire emblem book, casting the reader as Hippomenes, alchemical wisdom as Atalanta, and the fifty emblems as the golden apples that punctuate the pursuit.

Maier places the race of Hippomenes and Atalanta on the frontispiece of the Atalanta Fugiens, adjacent to the Garden of the Hesperides with its own golden apples guarded by a dragon. De Jong demonstrates that Maier deliberately fuses the two myths: the golden apples of Hippomenes and the golden apples of the Hesperides are identified as the same golden treasure, the philosophical gold that is both the means and the end of the alchemical work. The frontispiece thus presents two complementary images of the pursuit of wisdom: the race (a dynamic chase through time) and the garden (a static space of fulfilled promise guarded by danger).

In Maier's musical structure, the golden apple takes on a precise technical meaning. The three-voice fugues that accompany each emblem feature one voice fleeing (Atalanta/volatile mercury), one voice pursuing (Hippomenes/active sulphur), and a third voice, the cantus firmus, that brings the chase to rest. In the preface, Maier explicitly identifies this third voice with the golden apple: 'that third voice is simple and homogeneous in its value, like the golden apple.' The golden apple is thus the principle of resolution, the fixed element that reconciles the volatile dynamic of pursuit and flight. In alchemical terms, it is the philosophical gold, the stable product of the work that emerges when mercury and sulphur achieve equilibrium.

De Jong traces the alchemical interpretation of the Atalanta myth through Maier's broader program of mytho-alchemy. In the Arcana Arcanissima, Maier had already established his method of reading classical myths as concealed alchemical doctrine. The race of Atalanta and Hippomenes encodes the fundamental alchemical dynamic: the volatile principle (mercury, the feminine, the fleet Atalanta) must be captured and fixed by the active principle (sulphur, the masculine, the cunning Hippomenes), and the agent of fixation is gold itself (the golden apples, the philosopher's stone in embryonic form). The reader who chases meaning through the fifty emblems is enacting this pursuit: each emblem offers a golden apple of insight that momentarily arrests the flight of understanding before the chase resumes.

The golden apple also connects to the lusus serius, Maier's concept of serious play. The apples are distractions, but productive distractions: they slow the chase not to prevent its completion but to enable it. Atalanta's pause to pick up the apple is the moment of contemplation, the pause in which understanding crystallizes before the pursuit of further knowledge resumes. The entire Atalanta Fugiens is structured as a series of such pauses: each emblem arrests the reader's attention, invites meditation, and then releases the reader to continue the chase.

For the modern reader, the golden apple is the interpretive key to the Atalanta Fugiens as a whole. It explains why the book is structured as a sequence of fifty discrete units rather than a continuous treatise: each emblem is a golden apple, a moment of arrested beauty and meaning within the larger pursuit. It explains why the book is called 'Atalanta Fugiens' (the Fleeing Atalanta) rather than 'Hippomenes Victorious': the emphasis is on the chase itself, the dynamic process of pursuing wisdom, rather than on a static possession of it. And it explains why Maier added music to the emblem format: the three-voice fugue enacts the chase and its resolution in audible form, with the cantus firmus sounding the golden apple's note of rest within the restless counterpoint of pursuit and flight.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The fruit of Hesperides that Hippomenes drops to slow Atalanta: cipher of the perfected gold or Stone whose apparition arrests the volatile flight of mercurial matter.
Medical
The aurum potabile in fruit form; the perfected medicinal essence whose ingestion restores incorruptible health, figured as the food of the gods in classical legend.
Spiritual
The lure that draws the soul out of unconscious flight into conscious encounter; the precious insight whose appearance, though fleeting, redirects the seeker toward the goal.
Cosmological
The solar fruit ripened by celestial fire at the world's western boundary; the round perfection in which the heavens condense their virtue into edible, bounded form.
