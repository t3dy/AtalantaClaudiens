Isis FIGURE

Egyptian goddess who reassembles the dismembered Osiris; represents the lunar anima that restores the dissolved solar principle to wholeness.

Isis is the Egyptian goddess of magic, healing, and maternal devotion, wife and sister of Osiris, mother of Horus, and one of the most important deities in the Egyptian pantheon. Her cult spread throughout the Hellenistic and Roman worlds, and the mystery religions associated with her worship were among the most influential in the ancient Mediterranean. In the central Egyptian myth, Osiris is murdered and dismembered by his brother Typhon (Set), and Isis devotedly searches for, collects, and reassembles his scattered body parts, restoring him to a form of resurrected life — though the phallus, lost to the waves, can never be recovered.

In the Atalanta Fugiens, Isis appears as a principal figure in Emblem XLIV, whose motto reads: 'Typhon kills Osiris by a ruse, and after that he scatters his limbs far and wide, but the famous Isis collects them.' The epigram names her as 'the sister, wife and mother of Osiris' and notes that the phallus is lost, 'for the Sulphur, which produced Sulphur, is missing.' This triple relationship — sister, wife, and mother — encodes the alchemical principle that the feminine substance relates to the masculine in multiple registers simultaneously: as cognate (same origin), as partner (coniunctio), and as matrix (regenerative source).

De Jong identifies Isis as the lunar-feminine counterpart to Osiris's solar-masculine principle, and traces Maier's reading to Diodorus Siculus's Bibliotheca Historica, the principal classical source for the Osiris-Isis narrative in the discourse of Emblem XLIV (De Jong, pp. 273-278). Maier makes the analogy explicit: Sun-Moon corresponds to Osiris-Isis, and these in turn correspond to the two principles that are the starting point of the alchemical universe. Isis is identified with Luna, and her equivalents include Juno, Venus, the mother of Oedipus, Danae, Deidameia, Atalanta, Helena, Latona, Semele, Europa, Leda, Antiope, and Thalia — all representing the receptive, volatile, mercurial principle in various mythological guises.

The discourse further recounts that Isis and Osiris taught people the use and cultivation of plants, paralleling the civilizing roles of Ceres (agriculture) and Dionysus (viticulture). De Jong notes that Isis's connection with the origin of alchemy is ancient: she is invoked in the earliest Greek alchemical papyri, and her priesthood's secret knowledge — represented in Emblem XXXIX by the Sphinx statues guarding the Isis cult altars — provided Maier with a model for alchemical secrecy.

Alchemically, Isis represents the soul of Osiris — the animating, spiritual principle that survives the physical destruction of the solar body and has the power to reassemble it. In the Arcana Arcanissima, Maier states directly that 'Osiris is the raw material of the alchemical work, from which the gold medicine is made; Isis, the soul of Osiris, is changed into Osiris and that is the sign of perfection.' Typhon is the fiery spirit that pierces Osiris — the corrosive agent of dissolution. Isis's gathering of the dismembered parts represents the careful recombination phase that follows the violent putrefaction.

The lost phallus is a significant detail: it represents the generative Sulphur that cannot be recovered in its original form after dissolution. The reconstituted Osiris is not identical to the original but transformed — resurrected in a higher mode, missing the crude reproductive power but possessing instead the perfected spiritual tincture.

For a general reader, Isis embodies the restorative, patient labor that follows destruction. Where Typhon violently tears apart, Isis carefully gathers, reassembles, and heals. She represents the alchemist's faith that what has been dissolved can be reconstituted in a higher form — and that the feminine principle of devoted attention is essential to the Work.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The reassembler: Isis gathers Osiris's scattered members and restores them to wholeness, representing the careful recombination of separated alchemical components after dissolution.
Medical
The healing art personified: the patient, systematic work of gathering scattered vital forces and reintegrating them into a functioning bodily whole.
Spiritual
The devoted seeker who through love, persistence, and wisdom reconstitutes what chaos has fragmented; the feminine principle of restorative wholeness.
Cosmological
The veiled goddess of nature whose secrets are hidden from the profane; she governs the regenerative cycles of the natural world and the Nile's fertile inundation.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Maier presents Isis in Emblem XLIV as the feminine restorative principle who gathers the scattered parts of the slain Osiris, encoding the reintegration phase of the alchemical Work. De Jong connects this to the broader pattern of female figures in AF who preserve and reconstitute what the masculine principle alone cannot.
