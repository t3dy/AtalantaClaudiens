King Duenech FIGURE

Rex Duenech
Alchemical king figure suffering from melancholic disease. Cured through purification. Appears in XXVIII and XLVIII.

King Duenech is a figure drawn from Arabic alchemical allegory — his name derives from the Arabic word dueneg (also duenez or duenum), meaning vitriol. He appears in the Rosarium Philosophorum and related texts as a monarch afflicted with saturnine melancholy — an excess of black bile that renders him dropsical, lethargic, and near death. The allegory narrates his cure through a series of steam baths and purgative treatments administered by his physician Pharut, who strips the king of his disease through progressive stages of sweating, anointing, and revivification.

In the Atalanta Fugiens, King Duenech appears principally in Emblem XXVIII, though Maier connects his allegory to the broader king-cycle that includes Emblems XXIV, XXXI, and XLVIII. The picture of Emblem XXVIII represents Duenech sitting with a crown on his head in a small steam-bath with a perforated bottom, through which steam rises from a fire below. De Jong notes that the picture draws elements from both the Duenech allegory and the Merlini allegory, which presents a similar king-cure narrative with the dead king's remains placed in a vaulted space above a collecting vessel (De Jong, pp. 210-214).

The Duenech narrative, as De Jong recounts it from the source texts, proceeds through a carefully structured medical cure: Pharut places the melancholic king in a bed with white sheets and administers clear water, causing intense sweating that stains the sheets with dissolved black bile. A second bed is prepared with specific materials — feathers of a young black eagle for the bolster, swan's feathers for the pillow, a black sheet and white cover — and the king is heated and rubbed with evil-smelling oil until the bile spreads to the top of his head. Pharut watches for the whitening of Duenech's palate as the sign of progress. Finally, the exhausted monarch is revived in a third bed with water, oil, and sulphur. When Duenech looks at himself, he sees that he has been freed from melancholy and has new, healthy blood.

De Jong identifies this as one of the king allegories in which the entire transmutation process is summarized — a process that occurs simultaneously in chemical, medical, psychical, and cosmological registers (De Jong, p. 211). The melancholy from which Duenech suffers belongs to the planet Saturn — the cold, dark planet farthest from the sun. The work begins when Saturn is in opposition to the Sun: one begins at the beginning, of which melancholy, Saturn, lead, and the putrefactio are the symbols.

The epigram specifies that 'King Duenech shines with the weapons of the Green Lion.' The Rosarium Philosophorum states that the Stone cannot be prepared without 'the green, liquid Duenech, who seems to come into being in our minerals.' De Jong traces this green color to green vitriol, also called the Green Lion or Azoth. The Green Lion that devours the sun — a famous Rosarium image — means dissolving gold in green vitriol. The gold or philosopher's stone lies hidden within King Duenech, who shines with the weapons of this corrosive green agent.

Maier connects the Duenech and Merlini allegories to the Eleusinian, Orphean, and Egyptian mysteries, arguing that the theme is always the same: death, purification, and resurrection. The king must be seized, divided into extremely small parts, washed with fire and water, purged of deposits and superfluous things, and put together again — after which he rises young and stronger than before.

For a general reader, King Duenech offers the most explicitly medical version of the alchemical narrative: a sick king is cured through progressive purgation, and the cure simultaneously heals the metal, the body, and the soul. The allegory makes vivid the principle that the base matter's initial corruption (melancholy, blackness, lead) is not an obstacle but the necessary starting point from which transformation proceeds.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The melancholic king of the Turba Philosophorum who must be cured in the steam bath: an allegory of the base metal requiring dissolution and purification to yield its hidden gold.
Medical
The paradigmatic melancholic patient: his saturnine excess of black bile must be treated with moist heat (the steam bath) to restore humoral equilibrium.
Spiritual
The soul imprisoned by saturnine depression; the leaden weight of acedia that can only be lifted through the warmth of spiritual practice and purgative ordeal.
Cosmological
Saturn's influence made flesh: the heavy, cold, dry principle that must be warmed and moistened by solar and lunar forces to participate in cosmic harmony.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Maier uses the figure of King Duenech in Emblems XXVIII and XLVIII to dramatize the alchemical process as a medical cure, in which the king's melancholic illness (black bile, dropsy) requires purging through steam and potion. De Jong's 1964 article traces this figure to the Merlini allegory, while Pagel emphasizes the three-level physiological correspondence between the king's cure and metallurgical coctions.
