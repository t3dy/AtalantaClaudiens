Kronos / Saturn FIGURE

Saturnus
Titan who devours his children and vomits the stone; alchemical figure for dissolution, nigredo, and the prima materia.

Kronos (Roman Saturn) is the Titan of Greek mythology who ruled during the Golden Age, father of Zeus, Poseidon, Hera, and the other Olympian gods. Warned by a prophecy that one of his children would overthrow him, Kronos devoured each child at birth — until Rhea substituted a stone wrapped in swaddling clothes for the infant Zeus, who was raised in secret on Crete. When Zeus reached maturity, he forced Kronos to vomit up the stone and the swallowed children, then led the Olympians in the war that overthrew the Titans.

In alchemical tradition, Saturn is the planetary ruler of lead — the heaviest, basest, and most melancholic of the seven metals. His cold, dark, slow nature governs the nigredo, the first and most terrifying stage of the Work, where existing forms are dissolved into black, putrefying prima materia. Saturn's devouring of his children represents the universal solvent consuming all metals; his vomiting of the stone represents the philosopher's stone emerging from the dissolved mass. The stone that Rhea substituted for Zeus is, in this reading, literally the lapis philosophorum.

In the Atalanta Fugiens, Saturn governs the nigredo emblems and appears explicitly in the discourse of Emblem XII, where the allegory of Saturn being succeeded by his son Jupiter encodes the transition from the first stage (dissolution, lead, melancholy) to the second (purification, tin, expansion). De Jong notes that Maier informs us in the discourse of Emblem XXII that Jupiter 'unmans Saturn,' representing the active principle that overcomes and transforms the passive, leaden starting material (De Jong, pp. 240-241). The Adonis discourse (Emblem XLI) includes Sol's speech from the Rosarium where the Philosophical Sun identifies Saturn as 'the one to whom it is given to be contrary to me' — the destructive force that separates the Sun's limbs, initiating the dissolution that Isis (in the Osiris narrative) or Venus (in the Adonis narrative) must later repair.

Forshaw notes that Maier discusses Saturn vomiting up the stone as an alchemical myth, and that the devouring/vomiting cycle maps onto the laboratory process of dissolving gold in a strong acid and then precipitating it out again in purified form. The melancholy associated with Saturn connects to the King Duenech allegory of Emblem XXVIII, where the saturnine king must be cured of his black bile through progressive heating — the warmth of the work overcoming the cold darkness of the initial matter.

Saturn's planetary influence — coldness, blackness, slowness, heaviness — maps onto the first stage of every alchemical operation depicted in the series. The leaden weight of the prima materia, the putrefying blackness of the nigredo, and the melancholic temperament of the practitioner who contemplates the seemingly hopeless initial state are all governed by Saturn. Yet within Saturn's darkness lies the seed of gold, just as within lead the alchemists believed the potential for transmutation was hidden.

Maier's discourse in Emblem XLIV lists Saturn alongside Mercury and Vulcan as the three forces that recur on every page of alchemical writing: Saturn as father and cause of all things, Mercury as materia and forma, and Vulcan as the active force. This triad — the leaden origin, the mercurial medium, and the fiery agent — constitutes the essential dramatis personae of the alchemical stage.

For a general reader, Saturn represents the principle that transformation begins in darkness. The alchemist's first task is to confront the heaviest, blackest, most unpromising material — and to recognize that the stone the Titan vomited, the one mistaken for a worthless substitute, is in fact the treasure everyone has been seeking.
