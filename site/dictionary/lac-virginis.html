Lac Virginis SUBSTANCE

Virgin's milk — a volatile alchemical spirit considered a form of the quintessence, used in the first stage of creating the philosopher's stone.

Lac virginis, the "virgin's milk," is an alchemical term for a volatile, white, milky substance understood as a purified form of mercury or a mercurial preparation essential to the early stages of producing the Philosopher's Stone. The imagery of nurturing milk produced by a virgin connects the substance to deep currents in both alchemical and Christian symbolism, blending the practical chemistry of white precipitates with theological associations of purity, maternal nourishment, and sacrificial feeding. Albertus Magnus, one of the most important authorities in the tradition Maier inherits, provides the definitive statement: quicksilver "is the water of life, the virgin's milk, the fountain, the alum, from which whoever drinks does not die."

In the broader alchemical tradition, lac virginis belongs to a family of related feminine-coded substances including aqua vitae, aqua permanens (permanent water), and the philosophical mercury itself. All share the quality of being liquid, purifying, and life-giving, and all are associated with the feminine, receptive, lunar principle that must unite with the masculine, active, solar principle to produce the Stone. The "virginity" of the milk signifies that the substance has not yet been contaminated by combination with grosser materials: it is mercury in its most pristine, uncorrupted state, ready to receive the seed of philosophical gold.

Basil Valentine, in his Twelve Keys, provides a practical context: the Medicine and Stone of the Sages is prepared out of the "true virgin's milk," which is then combined with refined gold beaten into thin plates and subjected to gentle fire. This suggests that lac virginis referred to a specific mercurial preparation used as the solvent or menstruum in which gold was dissolved and from which the Stone was crystallized. The whiteness of the preparation is significant, marking it as a product of the albedo stage, the purification that follows the initial blackening of the nigredo.

In Atalanta Fugiens, the virgin's milk concept surfaces most provocatively in Emblem V, where a woman suckles a toad at her breast. De Jong traces this image to the Pseudo-Aristotelian Tractatulus de Practica Lapidis Philosophici, reading the woman as the mercurial body (the source of nourishing liquid) and the toad as the volatile sulphurous component that absorbs and is stabilized by the feeding. The milk that passes from woman to toad is the lac virginis in its active mode: the mercurial solvent being consumed in the process of fixing the volatile sulphur. That the woman dies from this feeding while the toad grows and matures encodes the alchemical principle that the solvent is sacrificed in the process of preparing the Stone.

Szulakowska adds an important theological dimension, identifying lac virginis as the alchemical concept underlying Maier's Apocalyptic Woman illustration in the Symbola Aureae Mensae. She reads the Virgin's life-giving fluid as having transformatory qualities analogous to the Communion wine, connecting the alchemical virgin's milk to the Eucharistic theology that she argues pervades Maier's work. The Fleece of Gideon, which De Jong mentions in her introduction, further connects the concept to Marian theology: just as pure dew descended from heaven into the Fleece of Gideon, so the purified matter rises as vapor and descends in condensed form in the alchemist's retort, and the Virgin conceived Christ in a manner analogous to this celestial dew.

In chemical terms, lac virginis most likely referred to various white precipitates of mercury, particularly mercurous chloride (calomel) or white mercuric oxide, which could be prepared by treating mercury solutions with various reagents. These white, milky-looking substances were genuine chemical products with real (if often dangerous) medicinal applications, and their visual resemblance to milk would have reinforced the symbolic association.

For the lay reader, lac virginis illustrates the characteristic alchemical fusion of laboratory observation and symbolic imagination. A white, milky chemical precipitate becomes the Virgin's milk, connecting a test tube to the mother of God, a chemical reaction to the mystery of the Incarnation. This is not confusion but deliberate polysemy: the alchemists believed that the same principles operated at every level of reality, and a substance that behaved like nurturing milk in the laboratory truly participated in the cosmic principle of maternal nourishment.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The Virgin's Milk: a name for the pure, white, mercurial water of the philosophers—the lactescent solvent that nourishes the embryonic Stone within the philosophical egg.
Medical
Whitened milk-tinctures and lactic preparations used as gentle nutritives for invalids and infants; the fluidum vitae whose purity restores depleted radical moisture.
Spiritual
The sustaining grace that feeds the newborn spiritual life; the pure, untainted nourishment given by the Sophianic feminine to the soul reborn after nigredo.
Cosmological
The lunar dew that descends as silver-white moisture in the cool of dawn; the maternal milk of the Moon that fertilizes terrestrial generation in classical natural philosophy.
