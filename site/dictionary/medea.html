Medea FIGURE

Sorceress of Greek mythology who rejuvenates old Aeson by boiling him in a cauldron; model for alchemical rejuvenation in Emblem IX.

Medea is the sorceress-princess of Colchis in Greek mythology, granddaughter of the sun-god Helios, famous for her mastery of pharmaceutical magic and her central role in Jason's quest for the Golden Fleece. Among her celebrated feats was the rejuvenation of Aeson, Jason's elderly father, whom she restored to youth by draining his blood and replacing it with a potion of herbs, minerals, and animal parts boiled in a cauldron. This act of magical medicine — dissolving an aged body and reconstituting it in a rejuvenated form — made Medea a natural emblem for the alchemical art.

In the Atalanta Fugiens, Medea's rejuvenation of Aeson connects principally to Emblem IX. Forshaw identifies the old man of Emblem IX — fixed to a tree in celestial dew, being restored to youth — as drawing on the same tradition of magical rejuvenation that Medea exemplifies, and connects the motif to Ficino's De Vita Libri Tres on curing melancholy and aging through astral-magical means. De Jong identifies the parallel between Medea's cauldron magic and the alchemical process of dissolution and reconstitution.

Maier's treatment of Medea extends beyond Emblem IX. In the discourse of Emblem XLIV, Medea appears in the comprehensive catalogue of mythological parallels that demonstrate the alchemical process: she had 'rejuvenated herself, as she had also rejuvenated Jason's father Aeson, just as Ceres rejuvenated Pelops, for which reason she was called the one twice rejuvenated.' The motif of twofold rejuvenation — first of others, then of herself — encodes the alchemical principle that the transformative agent (the menstruum or solvent) is itself transformed in the process of transforming its subject.

In the discourse of Emblem XXV, Maier identifies Jason as 'the physician' who succeeded in obtaining the Golden Fleece 'thanks to Medea's advice, that is to say thanks to the advice of Ratio and Theoretica.' Medea is thus identified not with a chemical substance but with theoretical knowledge — the rational understanding of nature that enables the practical adept (Jason) to succeed where brute strength fails. This reading is significant because it assigns Medea an intellectual rather than merely magical role: she is the wisdom that guides the Work, not just the potion that effects it.

Alchemically, Medea's cauldron represents the alchemical vessel in which the aged, impure body (philosophical gold in its debased state) is dissolved in a powerful menstruum (Medea's potion) and reconstituted in a purified, rejuvenated form. The draining of Aeson's blood and its replacement with the herbal-mineral preparation maps onto the process of extracting the mercury from the philosophical body and replacing it with a purified tincture. The mythological emphasis on rejuvenation rather than creation from scratch aligns with Maier's insistence that the alchemist does not create gold ex nihilo but transforms an existing impure substance into its perfected state.

For a general reader, Medea bridges the gap between magic and medicine in Maier's symbolic vocabulary. Her cauldron is both a witch's tool and a physician's instrument, and the rejuvenation she performs is simultaneously a fairy-tale enchantment and a technical chemical procedure. The sorceress who can make the old young again embodies the alchemist's most audacious promise: the reversal of decay, the restoration of vitality, the transmutation of base matter into gold.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The Colchian sorceress whose pharmaka enable Jason to seize the Fleece: figure of the chemical knowledge—the secret tinctures and washes—without which the operative artist accomplishes nothing.
Medical
The herbalist of dangerous arcana: her ointments anaesthetize the dragon and renew aged Aeson's blood, archetypes of the powerful, ambivalent remedies that iatrochemistry inherits.
Spiritual
The dark feminine wisdom whose knowledge the seeker must accept on her terms; the unconscious counsellor whose drugs both enable transformation and exact a tragic price.
Cosmological
The sublunary lunar feminine whose magical waters embody the moon's influence over plants, blood, and tides; the mediating Hecatean intelligence at the threshold of three worlds.
