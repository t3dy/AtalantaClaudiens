Mytho-Alchemy CONCEPT

Mytho-Alchemia
The practice of reading classical mythology as encoded alchemical teaching; Maier is identified by Forshaw as its 'main figurehead.'

Mytho-alchemy is a term coined by the scholar Peter Forshaw to describe the systematic interpretation of Greek, Egyptian, and Roman mythology as concealed alchemical doctrine. The practice rests on the Hermetic assumption that ancient myths were not naive fictions or entertaining stories but deliberate encodings of natural philosophical secrets, composed by sages who wished to transmit dangerous knowledge only to qualified readers. The myths served as a protective veil: their surface narrative of gods, heroes, and monsters entertained the uninitiated, while their hidden alchemical content was accessible only to those who possessed the interpretive key.

The mytho-alchemical tradition has ancient roots. The Stoic practice of allegorizing Homer and Hesiod as natural philosophy provided one precedent, and the late antique alchemists of Alexandria, including Zosimos, already connected mythological figures to chemical processes. The medieval Petrus Bonus, in his Margarita Pretiosa Novella (1330), argued explicitly that Homer, Virgil, and Ovid were alchemical authors writing in concealed form. But the full flowering of mytho-alchemy as a systematic hermeneutic came in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, when the Renaissance recovery of classical texts and the Hermetic revival associated with Marsilio Ficino created the conditions for a comprehensive mythological alchemy.

Maier is identified by Forshaw as the main figurehead of mytho-alchemy. His Arcana Arcanissima (1614) is the foundational text of the practice, offering sustained alchemical readings of the myths of Osiris, Atalanta, Hercules, Jason, and numerous other classical figures. The Atalanta Fugiens, published three years later, represents the visual and musical culmination of this method, transforming fifty mythological and allegorical scenes into an integrated alchemical curriculum. Every emblem takes a mythological, biblical, or allegorical scene and reveals its alchemical content: the race of Atalanta encodes the volatile-fixed dynamic, the labors of Hercules encode the stages of the opus, the killing and reassembly of Osiris encodes dissolution and reintegration.

De Jong's source-critical method demonstrates empirically what Forshaw's term captures theoretically. By tracing each motto and discourse back to its sources in the Turba Philosophorum, the Rosarium, Ovid, Hesiod, Strabo, and Diodorus Siculus, De Jong shows that Maier systematically reads myth as chemistry. The mottos of emblems with mythological themes are drawn from classical sources, but the explanations in the discourses are grounded in alchemical authorities, creating a deliberate fusion of the two registers.

Maier's mytho-alchemy is not arbitrary allegorizing. It follows a consistent logic in which the narrative structure of a myth maps onto the temporal sequence of the alchemical work. The hero's descent into danger corresponds to the nigredo; the struggle with a monster or adversary corresponds to the dissolution of the prima materia; the hero's triumph and return correspond to the achievement of the lapis. This structural consistency across dozens of myths constitutes Maier's argument that all mythology, properly understood, teaches the same alchemical truth.

For the modern reader, mytho-alchemy is the key to understanding why the Atalanta Fugiens is populated with figures from Greek and Egyptian mythology rather than with laboratory equipment. Maier believed that the gods and heroes of antiquity were themselves alchemists, or at least that the stories told about them encoded alchemical knowledge. To read the Atalanta Fugiens is to enter a world where Oedipus, Isis, Hercules, and Atalanta are all participants in the same Great Work.

In Atalanta Fugiens

The entire AF is an exercise in mytho-alchemy. Every emblem takes a mythological, biblical, or allegorical scene and reveals its alchemical content — the race of Atalanta, the labors of Hercules, the killing of Osiris, the riddle of the Sphinx. Forshaw's term captures what De Jong's source-critical method demonstrates empirically: Maier systematically reads myth as chemistry.
