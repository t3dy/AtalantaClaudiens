Osiris FIGURE

Egyptian god of death and resurrection; in alchemical allegory, represents the solar principle that must be dismembered and reconstituted.

Osiris is the Egyptian god of the dead and the afterlife, the divine king who ruled Egypt during a mythological Golden Age before being murdered by his brother Typhon (the Egyptian Set). In the central Egyptian myth, Typhon kills Osiris by ruse, dismembers his body, and scatters the limbs throughout Egypt. Isis, Osiris's wife and sister, devotedly searches for and collects the scattered parts, reassembling them and restoring Osiris to a form of resurrected existence in the underworld, where he rules as lord of the dead. The annual Nile flood was understood as the tears of Isis mourning Osiris, and the agricultural cycle of death and rebirth was identified with his resurrection.

In the Atalanta Fugiens, Osiris is the subject of Emblem XLIV, which De Jong identifies as one of the most important and densely argued emblems in the entire series (De Jong, pp. 273-278). The motto reads: 'Typhon kills Osiris by a ruse, and after that he scatters his limbs far and wide, but the famous Isis collects them.' The epigram establishes the equivalence: 'Syria has Adonis, Greece has Dionysus, Egypt has Osiris, who is nobody else but the Sun of Wisdom.' The identification of Osiris with the Philosophical Sun — not the celestial sun but the solar principle hidden within matter — is the emblem's foundational claim.

Maier's discourse draws extensively on Diodorus Siculus's Bibliotheca Historica as the principal classical source. Maier states emphatically that whatever arguments might be advanced to convince him that Osiris was a god or king of the Egyptians, he would not believe it — Osiris is the Philosophical Sun, and the common people merely mistook the philosophical symbol for a literal deity. The discourse then unfolds an extraordinary catalogue of mythological parallels: Sol is simultaneously Osiris, Dionysus, Bacchus, Jupiter, Mars, Adonis, Oedipus, Perseus, Achilles, Triptolemus, Pelops, Hippomenes, and Pollux. Luna is simultaneously Isis, Juno, Venus, Atalanta, Helena, Latona, and others. The masters of art are Hercules, Ulysses, Jason, Theseus, and Pirithous.

De Jong identifies the death and resurrection of Osiris as Maier's symbol for the whole alchemical process. Osiris killed by Typhon corresponds to death and putrefaction — the nigredo. Osiris's members scattered far and wide represent the return to primary matter and inclusion in the circulation process of coming into existence and perishing in Nature. Isis collecting the limbs represents the careful reassembly phase. The killed Osiris rising represents the conclusion of the cycle — the completion of the Work.

In the Arcana Arcanissima, Maier provides an explicit key: Osiris is the raw material of the alchemical work, from which the gold medicine is made. Isis, the soul of Osiris, is changed into Osiris, and that is the sign of perfection. Typhon is the fiery spirit that pierces Osiris — the corrosive agent of dissolution. This three-part schema — the solar body (Osiris), the lunar soul (Isis), and the fiery agent (Typhon) — maps onto the standard alchemical triad of philosophical Sulphur, philosophical Mercury, and the fire that drives the transformation.

The lost phallus of Osiris, noted in the epigram ('the Sulphur, which produced Sulphur, is missing'), represents the generative power that cannot be recovered in its original form after dissolution. The reconstituted Osiris is transformed — no longer the crude fertile substance but the perfected, spiritual gold.

Forshaw connects the Osiris-Typhon-Isis narrative to Maier's broader mytho-alchemical project, where Egyptian, Greek, Syrian, and Roman mythologies all encode the same chemical process. The Emblem XLIV discourse demonstrates this method at its most ambitious, establishing equivalences across entire pantheons.

For a general reader, Osiris embodies the alchemical faith that death is not the end but a necessary stage of transformation. The dismembered god, patiently reassembled by devoted Isis, becomes more powerful than before — not the same Osiris, but a resurrected one, ruling a higher kingdom.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The dismembered king whose body is scattered and must be reassembled by Isis: an allegory of the dissolution and reconstitution of matter in the Great Work. Maier connects Typhon's putrefaction of Osiris directly to the alchemical process.
Medical
The diseased body fragmented by illness whose scattered parts (organs, humors) must be gathered and reintegrated by the healing art.
Spiritual
The divine self torn apart by the forces of chaos (Typhon) and reassembled through love and devotion (Isis); death and resurrection as spiritual paradigm.
Cosmological
The solar deity whose annual death and resurrection mirrors the agricultural cycle of seed, growth, harvest, and renewal—the cosmic rhythm of dying and rising gods.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Maier deploys Osiris in Emblem XLIV as the dying solar god whose dismemberment by Typhon and reassembly by Isis encodes the alchemical process of dissolution and reconstitution. De Jong reads this Egyptian myth as Maier's figure for the death and resurrection of the philosophical Sun.
