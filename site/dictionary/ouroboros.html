Ouroboros FIGURE

The ouroboros, the serpent or dragon that devours its own tail, is among the oldest and most universal symbols in human culture. It appears in Egyptian funerary texts, in Gnostic cosmology, in Norse mythology as Jormungandr, and in Hindu and Buddhist iconography. The image conveys cyclical time, self-renewal, the unity of beginning and end, and the paradox of self-consumption that generates rather than destroys. In the Greek alchemical tradition, the ouroboros appears as early as the Chrysopoeia of Cleopatra (circa 300 AD), a manuscript from the Leiden Papyrus in which the serpent encircles the words "The One is the All."

In alchemy, the ouroboros is the master symbol of the unity of matter and the circular nature of the alchemical process. De Jong explains that from the earliest alchemical texts, the ouroboros represents the unity of matter under two opposite aspects: "draco est nigredo" (the dragon is the blackening) and "draco est aqua divina" (the dragon is the divine water). On one hand the dragon symbolizes black matter in the stage of destruction and decomposition; on the other it is the life-giving rain. In the initial stage it is poison; in the final stage it is the means of salvation. The dragon bites its own tail because the sevenfold purification transforms the perishable into the imperishable, closing the circle of the work.

Maier treats the ouroboros in Emblem XIV of the Atalanta Fugiens, embedding it within a discourse that connects the self-devouring serpent to the circular course of the year and the seasonal rhythm of the alchemical work. According to Maier, the Chemists connected the dragon devouring its tail with their work, and by the dragon they indicated sulphur, but Maier did not exclude the circular course of the year as an additional meaning. In the discourse to Emblem XLV, Maier prescribes beginning work when the Sun is in Capricorn, completing the first stage when the Sun stands in Aries, continuing the women's work until Leo, and proceeding until the year seizes its tail with its head like a snake. The ouroboros thus becomes a calendar for the opus, aligning the laboratory process with cosmic time.

De Jong traces the source of the ouroboros imagery to the Tractatus Aureus attributed to Hermes Trismegistus and to Lambsprinck's emblems, where the motto reads: "This is truly a great wonder and a swift cunning, that the highest medicine is found in the poisonous Dragon." She identifies the chemical meaning as Mercury, properly precipitated or sublimated, dissolved in its own water and coagulated again. De Jong also connects the alchemical dragon to the Gnostic dragon of outermost darkness from the Pistis Sophia, which separates this world from the outer world and must be destroyed before the lower world can be redeemed. The dragon of the Atalanta Fugiens thus operates simultaneously as chemical instruction (the repeated cycle of dissolution and coagulation), cosmological symbol (the year devouring itself), and spiritual allegory (the destruction of chaos by the light of Sol and Luna).

The ouroboros may be the single image that best captures Maier's vision of the alchemical work as a lusus serius, a serious game of infinite circularity. The serpent that devours itself is both horrifying and generative, both poison and medicine. Maier asks his reader to hold these contradictions together, to see in the circular dragon not a vicious circle but a productive one: the repeated solve et coagula, the seasonal return of the sun, and the spiritual death and rebirth that alchemists believed mirrored the passion of Christ and the renewal of the cosmos.
