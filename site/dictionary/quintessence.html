Quintessence SUBSTANCE

Quinta Essentia

The quintessence, or quinta essentia, is the fifth element of the philosophical tradition, the substance that transcends and perfects the four terrestrial elements of earth, water, air, and fire. Aristotle introduced the concept as the aether, the incorruptible material of the celestial spheres, distinct from the changeable elements of the sublunary world. In alchemical appropriation, the quintessence became the goal of the highest operations: a perfectly purified substance in which the four elements had been brought to such a degree of fusion and refinement that they produced something qualitatively new, something that partook of celestial nature while remaining accessible to human art.

Within the alchemical tradition, the quintessence occupied a position of supreme importance as the active principle that could perfect imperfect matter. Raymond Lully devoted an entire treatise to the Quinta Essentia, describing methods for extracting the fifth essence from wine, herbs, and minerals through repeated distillation and circulation. The quintessence was understood not as a fifth substance added to the other four but as the hidden perfection latent within them, released through the art of the alchemist. When the four elements within a substance were purified and brought to perfect equilibrium, the quintessence emerged spontaneously, just as a perfectly balanced body produces health and a perfectly tuned instrument produces harmony.

In the Atalanta Fugiens, the quintessence appears in connection with Emblem VIII, where the philosophical egg contains all four elements in unified form, and Emblem XXXVII, whose motto names three requirements for mastery: white smoke, the green lion, and stinking water. De Jong traces the concept through Maier's sources, noting that the Tabula Smaragdina itself, which provides the motto for the opening emblem, was understood as expressing the quintessence of all natural changes. The Emerald Tablet's teaching that the substance ascends from earth to heaven and descends again, receiving the force of things above and below, describes precisely the process by which the quintessence is extracted: the volatile rises, the fixed remains, and their reunion produces something that transcends both.

De Jong identifies passages in which Maier draws on the Lullian tradition of the quinta essentia, noting that Lullius prescribed the warmth of dung for his quintessence and that the various menstrua discussed in the Atalanta Fugiens are stages in its extraction. The discourse of Emblem XVII, with its detailed classification of four fires, describes the heat regime necessary to bring the four elements into the state of perfect equilibrium from which the quintessence emerges. The salt of the Pythagoreans, discussed by De Jong in relation to later emblems, was identified with the quintessence on account of its bitterness and its role as the binding principle that holds the other elements together.

The quintessence connects to Maier's broader philosophical framework through the concept of the philosopher's stone, which many alchemical authorities identified as the quinta essentia perfected. The stone's ability to transmute base metals and cure all diseases was attributed to its quintessential nature: it had transcended the limitations of the four mundane elements and could therefore impose perfection upon anything it contacted. In Maier's multi-register symbolism, the quintessence operates as material product, medical panacea, spiritual perfection, and cosmic principle simultaneously.

For the modern reader, the quintessence is best understood as the alchemists' answer to the question: what would a perfectly pure substance be like? Not earth, not water, not air, not fire, but something containing and transcending all four, the quintessence was the theoretical limit of the alchemist's art. In the Atalanta Fugiens, it is the unnamed destination toward which every emblem's allegory of purification, dissolution, and reunion ultimately points.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The fifth essence: the incorruptible substrate distilled out of the four elements, equated by adepts with the Stone's purest form and the perfected matter that grounds all transmutation.
Medical
The vital pneuma or radical moisture that animates the body; the subtle medium whose extraction and re-administration constitutes the spagyrist's universal medicine.
Spiritual
The incorruptible kernel of the soul that survives every dissolution; the imperishable witness whose recovery is the goal of contemplative work in the Hermetic tradition.
Cosmological
Aristotle's aether: the supralunary substance of the celestial spheres, weightless and unchanging, whose downward refraction into terrestrial matter is what alchemy strives to capture.
