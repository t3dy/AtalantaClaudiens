Rex FIGURE

The King figure representing Sol, gold, or the masculine fixed principle in alchemical allegory.

The Rex (King) is the primary masculine persona of alchemical allegory, representing Sol (the Sun), gold, the fixed principle, and the active, sulphurous agent of transformation. Paired with the Regina (Queen) as his feminine counterpart, the King's narrative arc — sickness, dissolution, death, and resurrection — encodes the entire alchemical process in miniature. In the planetary-metallic correspondence system, the King corresponds to the Sun and gold; his elemental qualities are hot and dry, and his humoral temperament is choleric.

In the Atalanta Fugiens, the King appears across a sustained four-emblem cycle that De Jong and Wescott identify as one of the work's most coherent narrative sequences. In Emblem XXIV, the wolf devours the King — which Forshaw, citing Lawrence Principe, identifies as antimony purifying gold. The King is thrown into the fire from which he rises crowned. In Emblem XXVIII, the King appears as Duenech, the melancholic monarch cured in the steam bath. In Emblem XXXI, the sea-king is in danger of drowning and promises great treasure to whoever saves him. In Emblem XLVIII, the Merlini king dissolves in water and rises as the philosopher's stone.

The king allegory tradition, from which Maier draws, has deep roots in Arabic alchemy. De Jong traces the Duenech and Merlini allegories to the Turba Philosophorum and related texts, and notes that Maier deems them equivalent to the Eleusinian, Orphean, and Egyptian mysteries (De Jong, pp. 210-214). The theme is always death, purification, and resurrection: 'Our king should be seized and divided into extremely small parts, be washed with fire and water, and — after deposits and superfluous things having been removed — be put together again, after which he rises, young and stronger than before.'

The Rosarium Philosophorum, the AF's probable structural model, culminates with an image of the philosopher's stone compared to the risen Son of God, represented by Christ rising from a sarcophagus. The identification of the alchemical King with Christ's death and resurrection is not Maier's invention but a tradition he inherits and deploys. The King's suffering and cure encode what Szulakowska identifies as the alchemical Eucharist — the identification of chemical transmutation with the central Christian mystery.

Alchemically, the King represents the Sulphur principle — the fixed, solar, active agent that must be dissolved (killed) before it can be purified and reconstituted in a more perfect form. The wolf that devours him in Emblem XXIV is antimony, the corrosive agent that dissolves impure gold. The steam bath that cures him in Emblem XXVIII is the gentle, moist heat that drives off the melancholic impurities. The water that threatens to drown him in Emblem XXXI is the mercurial solvent in which the fixed substance must be immersed. And the dissolution in Emblem XLVIII is the final liquefaction from which the stone emerges.

The King's color symbolism tracks the stages of the Work: he begins in Saturn's black (nigredo), passes through the white of purification (albedo), and achieves the triumphant red of the rubedo — the Red King united with the White Queen in the perfected stone. Wescott's musical analysis suggests that the modal structures of the fugues accompanying the King emblems encode these color transitions.

For a general reader, the Rex is the central character of the alchemical drama — the substance that must suffer, die, and be reborn. His royal status signals that the material being worked is the noblest of metals (gold), and his narrative of sickness and cure translates the laboratory process into a story of royal heroism. The alchemist is simultaneously the King's physician, executioner, and resurrector.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The Red King: philosophical Sulphur, the solar-masculine principle that must be united with the White Queen (Mercury) to produce the Stone. In Emblem XXIV, the king is cured of melancholy.
Medical
The dominant vital principle or ruling humor; in Maier's allegory, King Duenech's melancholic sickness represents the imbalance that the art must cure.
Spiritual
The conscious will and ruling ego whose saturnine fixity must be dissolved before it can be reborn in a higher, more integrated form.
Cosmological
The Sun as cosmic sovereign; the kingly principle of order, authority, and formative power that governs the celestial hierarchy.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Maier deploys the King figure across a sustained four-emblem sequence (XXIV, XXVIII, XXXI, XLVIII) that De Jong and Wescott identify as the Alchemical King cycle, dramatizing the sickness, death, and resurrection of the solar principle. The King's suffering and cure encode the entire Work in miniature, from nigredo through rubedo.
