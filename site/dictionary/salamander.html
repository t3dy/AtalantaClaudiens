Salamander FIGURE

Salamandra

The salamander is one of the most enduring creatures of pre-modern natural history, believed since antiquity to be capable of living in fire without being consumed. Pliny, Aristotle, and medieval bestiaries all described this small, lizard-like amphibian as possessing a cold, moist constitution so extreme that it could extinguish flames by its mere presence. Maier himself reports seeing salamanders in the Alps, emerging from chasms after thunderstorms, where farmers called them "Molch." He notes that the animal is surrounded by a slimy fluid that protects it from fire, but he is careful to distinguish this natural salamander from its philosophical counterpart.

In alchemical tradition, the salamander represents the Philosophers' Stone in its final, perfected state: the substance that has been so thoroughly hardened and balanced in its elemental composition that fire cannot destroy it. The Philosophical Salamander, unlike the common animal, is born in fire rather than merely surviving it. Its nature is hot and dry, the opposite of the cold, wet natural salamander. Avicenna's Tractatulus de Alchimia provides the key formulation that the alchemists adopted: the philosophers have called their stone the Salamander, because like a salamander it is fed exclusively by fire and lives in it, that is to say, is perfected by it.

Maier makes the salamander the subject of Emblem XXIX, whose motto states simply: "Just as the Salamander, the stone lives in the fire." The engraved plate depicts the salamander resting contentedly in flames while the figure of Vulcan, the divine smith, threatens it in vain. The epigram contrasts the cold natural salamander, which extinguishes fire and escapes unharmed, with the Philosophical Salamander, which is hot and is further helped by an equal heat. The discourse develops an elaborate argument about the four elements: animals can live in water and air, but earth and fire seem to exclude animal life. Only the salamander contradicts this rule. The Philosophical Salamander, Maier argues, has its four elements equally balanced, and when fire tries to convert one element, its opposite prevents the conversion, creating an equilibrium that fire cannot overcome.

De Jong identifies the Salamander as the Mercury substance in its final stage, in its state of perfect indestructibility, where the executioner Vulcan has no hold on it any more. She traces the emblem to Avicenna's Tractatulus and notes that Geber's text in the Rosarium describes the Philosophers' Stone as needing to be formed into the nature of the Salamander by a hardening process, achieving extreme solidity so that fire can be resisted. De Jong connects Emblem XXIX to Emblem XXXV (Achilles and Triptolemus), which also deals with obtaining the Salamander nature through a process of habituation, and to Lambsprinck's emblem of the salamander, which carries the motto: "The Salamander lived in the fire, and the fire changed the Salamander into the most excellent colour."

The salamander emblem captures one of Maier's most appealing pedagogical paradoxes: the creature that lives in the very element that destroys all others. For the reader puzzling over the Atalanta Fugiens, the salamander is a riddle whose answer reveals the goal of the entire work. The Stone, like the salamander, is not merely resistant to fire but is perfected by it. Maier transforms a piece of natural-historical folklore into an image of alchemical triumph, the moment when the work passes beyond all danger of destruction and achieves the permanence that the adept has sought through every stage of dissolution, purification, and recombination.
