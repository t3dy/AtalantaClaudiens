Screech Owl FIGURE

Noctua

The owl has been a creature of ambivalent symbolism since antiquity. In Greek tradition, the owl was sacred to Athena, goddess of wisdom, and the little owl (Athene noctua) appeared on Athenian coins as a symbol of knowledge and discernment. Yet the owl was also a creature of the night, associated with death omens, darkness, and the uncanny. Roman augury regarded the screech owl (strix) as inauspicious. This duality, wisdom concealed in darkness, sight that penetrates the night, made the owl a natural figure for alchemical symbolism, where the most precious knowledge is hidden in obscurity and the blackest stage of the work conceals the seed of illumination.

In alchemical tradition, the owl or noctua is less commonly discussed than the raven or eagle, but it carries specific significance as a bird of the nigredo, the dark initial stage of the work. The owl sees in darkness where others are blind, just as the alchemist must perceive the hidden potential in the black, putrefying matter. Forshaw, discussing Maier's "alchemical bestiary" in his analysis of the Atalanta Fugiens, notes Maier's singular fascination with birds, pointing to the owl as the bird of Pallas Athena, goddess of wisdom, which is accused by the other birds in a courtroom drama that Maier draws from classical fable.

Maier treats the owl most prominently in connection with Emblem XLIII, which is devoted to the philosophical vulture but engages the broader family of birds that populate the Atalanta Fugiens. The discourse of Emblem XLIII describes birds that imitate human speech, parrots, ravens, jackdaws, and woodpeckers, before turning to the philosophical vulture that speaks not by habituation but by nature. Forshaw describes a scene from Maier's wider works in which various birds, including the raven, accuse the owl in a court case; the owl is vindicated in the end, its wisdom confirmed despite the hostility of its peers. The owl's nocturnal vision connects it to the raven that flies without wings in the dark of night and in the clear light of the afternoon, both of which are aspects of the single Philosophical Bird that proclaims itself black, white, yellow, and red.

De Jong's analysis of Emblem XLIII connects the putrefactio, represented by a black crow with clipped wings at the foot of a mountain, to the broader symbolism of nocturnal birds in the alchemical tradition. The owl shares with the raven and the vulture the capacity to operate in darkness, and De Jong notes that the vulture and raven symbolize the circular motion of the alchemical process: the black raven represents the putrefactio, and the vulture is the completion of it. The owl, as Athena's bird, adds the dimension of wisdom to this dark symbolism: the adept who can see in the nigredo's darkness, like the owl, possesses the sophia necessary to complete the work.

Maier's treatment of the owl reflects his broader engagement with birds as vehicles for alchemical instruction. Each bird in the Atalanta Fugiens represents a different mode of perception: the eagle soars, the raven endures darkness, the vulture speaks truth, and the owl sees what others cannot. The reader who encounters these avian figures is invited to adopt each bird's perspective in turn, learning to see the alchemical work from multiple vantage points. The owl's particular lesson is that wisdom dwells in darkness, that the most valuable insight comes not from the bright clarity of the rubedo but from patient observation during the black night of the nigredo.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The night-bird of Saturn whose cry punctuates nigredo: emblematic companion of the dying king and of melancholy contemplation, signaling the long darkness from which the work begins.
Medical
The prognostic of saturnine illness: the owl as omen of black-bile crisis, the diagnostic sign that the patient has entered the depressive phase requiring patience rather than intervention.
Spiritual
The wisdom of the dark; the contemplative who, like the owl, sees in night what the daylit mind cannot perceive—a wisdom acquired only by enduring the long sojourn in obscurity.
Cosmological
Athena's bird in its saturnine aspect: the celestial creature whose habits map onto the cold, slow, outermost planet, perceiving from that elevation what nearer luminaries miss.
