Solutio PROCESS

Dissolving of a solid into liquid; the return of differentiated matter to a fluid, undifferentiated state.

Solutio, the dissolving, is the operation of returning a solid, differentiated substance to a fluid, undifferentiated state — the liquefaction that the alchemists identified as the necessary first step of all transformative work. Where dissolution (dissolutio) describes the general breaking down of form, solutio specifically denotes the liquid medium in which this breaking down occurs: the solid body is dissolved in the philosophical water, the mercurial menstruum, or the universal solvent that the alchemists called their aqua. The result is a solution — a uniform liquid in which the formerly distinct components have lost their separate identities and returned to a state of primordial unity.

The Turba Philosophorum, the foundational text of the Latin alchemical tradition, teaches that the Work begins in liquefaction. This principle establishes solutio as the inaugural operation of the opus, the gateway through which all matter must pass before it can be reconstituted in perfected form. The alchemists' philosophical water was not common water but a specially prepared solvent — the mercury of the philosophers — which had the power to dissolve gold, silver, and all metals without destroying their essential natures. The dissolution was understood to be reversible: what has been dissolved can be coagulated again, and the alternation between solutio and coagulatio (solve et coagula) drives the entire cyclical progression of the Work.

In Atalanta Fugiens, Maier figures solutio through imagery of drowning, melting, immersion, and liquefaction. Emblem V presents the reciprocal logic of dissolution and coagulation: as the toad coagulates and grows, the woman dissolves and dies, exemplifying the axiom that every solution is a mortification. De Jong identifies the woman's death as a solutio — the liquid, maternal, mercurial principle is consumed in the act of nourishing and fixing the dry, earthy, sulphurous principle. The philosophical water washes and coagulates simultaneously, as the tradition teaches.

Emblem XXXI presents the Chemical King sinking into the deep waters of the aqua pontica, one of the forms in which Mercury appears. The king's immersion represents the solutio of the royal, solar, fixed principle in the mercurial solvent — a necessary dissolution that the king must endure before he can be reconstituted in perfected form. De Jong traces this imagery through the Duenech and Merlini allegories, where the king is repeatedly dissolved, divided into extremely small parts, and washed with fire and water before rising again young and stronger than before.

The final emblem, Emblem L, presents the ultimate solutio in the mutual dissolution of the woman and the serpent in each other's blood. De Jong identifies this as the culminating dissolution that completes the Work: after the contraries have been joined, they must dissolve into each other completely, losing all separate identity, before the perfected Stone can emerge from their union. The comprehensive list of operations in Emblem XXXIX places solutio first in the sequence — one should begin with dissolution, after which one coagulates, sublimates, distills, calcines, and fixes.

De Jong emphasizes that Maier, following the Scala Philosophorum and the Rosarium, treats dissolving and coagulating as the two main operations of the art. Dissolving and purifying are the twin pillars advanced in Emblems III, XI, and XIII. The discourse of Emblem XV explicitly quotes the clause solve et coagula from the Scala Philosophorum, establishing the alternation between dissolution and coagulation as the foundational rhythm of the entire Work.

For the non-specialist, solutio is the alchemical act of returning to zero — dissolving the existing form so that its components can be recombined in a better arrangement. It is the equivalent of melting down a flawed casting to pour it anew, of unraveling a tangled weave to re-weave it correctly, or of allowing a broken bone to heal by first reducing the fragments to their proper alignment. The alchemists understood that the quality of the final product depends entirely on the completeness of the initial dissolution.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The dissolving of solid matter into liquid form using the philosophical mercury; the body must be reduced to its primordial water before reconstitution.
Medical
The liquefaction of hardened deposits and obstructions in the body; restoring the natural flow of humors through dissolution of morbid concretions.
Spiritual
The melting of the hardened heart; emotional release and the surrender of rigid ego-structures to the transformative waters of the unconscious.
Cosmological
The universal solvent principle, the action of the primordial waters from which all forms originally emerged and to which they cyclically return.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Maier figures solutio through the imagery of drowning, melting, and immersion that recurs across the emblem sequence, where solid forms must be returned to liquid before they can be reconstituted in perfected form. De Jong connects this to the Turba's teaching that the Work begins in liquefaction.
