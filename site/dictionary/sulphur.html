Sulphur SUBSTANCE

The fixed, masculine, solar principle. Paired with Mercury as the two fundamental alchemical substances.

Sulphur, in alchemical philosophy, denotes the fixed, masculine, solar, active principle that pairs with Mercury as one of the two fundamental substances from which all metals are composed. Like Mercury, philosophical sulphur is not identical to the common yellow mineral element known by the same name; rather, it is a hypothetical principle whose nature common sulphur most closely approximates. De Jong emphasizes this distinction, citing Jabir: "By mercury and sulphur he did not understand common mercury or common sulphur, but a hypothetical substance, the nature of which was most closely approximated by sulphur and mercury." This theoretical sulphur represented the properties hot and dry, the active and fixing force that, when properly balanced with the cold-moist properties of mercury, produced the perfect metal, gold.

The Mercury-Sulphur theory originated in Arabic alchemy, particularly in the Jabir corpus from the eighth century, and was transmitted to Western Europe through the Latin Geber. According to this theory, metals were formed in the earth under the influence of the planets from the union of sulphur and mercury. The differences between metals were caused by the different proportions in which these two principles were mixed and by the purity of each component. Gold represented the perfect equilibrium; lead, tin, copper, and iron represented varying degrees of imbalance and impurity. Transmutation required adjusting these proportions, which in practice meant adding the missing qualities to imperfect metals.

Maier made this theory the structural principle of Atalanta Fugiens. As De Jong states definitively: "Maier wanted to express this sulphur-mercury theory, in the title of his emblem book, in the form of the legend of Atalanta and Hippomenes. Atalanta represents the volatile mercury, Hippomenes symbolizes the force of sulphur. It is the alchemist's task to unite these opposites." The frontispiece, which shows Hippomenes pursuing Atalanta with golden apples, is therefore a visual diagram of the Sulphur-Mercury relationship: the fixed, active sulphur principle chases and ultimately captures the volatile, receptive mercury principle.

In Emblem I, the foundational emblem sourced from the Tabula Smaragdina, Maier explicitly identifies the chemical meaning of the Son of Philosophy: "In chemical respect it is the sulphur, which is carried in mercury," as Lullius states. The sulphur-embryo is engendered by the heavenly Sun (the solar-sulphur principle) and the North Wind (the mercurial principle), and in due time produces twins: one white-haired (Calais, representing the albedo) and one red-haired (Zetes, representing the rubedo). De Jong reads the color symbolism as encoding the two stages of perfection that sulphur must achieve.

The Harpies whom the sons of Boreas expel are identified by De Jong as "the destroying sulphur," the impure, volatile form of the sulphur principle that must be driven away before perfection can emerge from the noxious vapors. This distinction between pure and impure sulphur parallels the distinction between philosophical and common mercury: only the purified form of each principle is useful for the Work.

In Emblems VII and XVI, the contrast between wingless and winged figures encodes the Sulphur-Mercury opposition. The wingless figure represents Sulphur in its fixed, grounded, solar character, while the winged figure represents Mercury in its volatile, ascending, lunar character. De Jong notes that this contrast recurs throughout the emblem sequence (Emblems VII, XVI, XLIII), always signifying the fundamental tension between fixation and volatility that the alchemist must resolve.

Paracelsus modified the Mercury-Sulphur theory by adding a third principle, Salt, corresponding with body (while Sulphur corresponded with spirit and Mercury with soul). De Jong notes that Paracelsus "took three principles: sulphur, mercury and sal, corresponding with spirit, soul and body, but he also stuck to the four elements fire, earth, air and water." Maier's work, however, follows Tilton's observation that it is based on the prisca sapientia rather than Paracelsian Tria Prima, maintaining the classical two-principle framework.

In chemical terms, common sulphur's property of burning with a characteristic flame and producing acrid fumes made it a natural representative of the hot, dry, active principle. Its role in numerous chemical reactions, particularly in combination with metals to form colored sulphides, gave empirical support to the theory that sulphur was a constituent of all metals.

For the museum visitor, Sulphur is the pursuer in the alchemical drama, the active force that chases, captures, and fixes the volatile Mercury. When you see Hippomenes running after Atalanta in the frontispiece of Atalanta Fugiens, you are seeing Sulphur in pursuit of Mercury, the fixed chasing the volatile, the masculine seeking the feminine, in the oldest and most fundamental story that alchemy tells.

Meanings Across Registers

De Jong shows that alchemical terms operate simultaneously across material, medical, spiritual, and cosmological dimensions.

Alchemical
The philosophical Sulphur: the hot, dry, masculine, combustible principle in metals—the active seed that gives form and color to the metallic body.
Medical
The vital heat or innate calor that drives digestion, growth, and generation; the choleric fire that, in proper measure, sustains bodily life.
Spiritual
The soul's desire and will; the active, formative principle of consciousness that shapes raw experience into meaning and purpose.
Cosmological
The solar and fiery principle; Sulphur corresponds to the Sun's generative heat that activates all earthly matter through its formative rays.

In Atalanta Fugiens

Maier consistently figures Sulphur as the fixed, pursuing principle, embodied in the frontispiece by Hippomenes who chases Atalanta with golden apples as catalytic lures. De Jong demonstrates that the wingless figures in Emblems VII and XVI represent Sulphur's solar, masculine nature, which must bond with its volatile counterpart to complete the Work.
