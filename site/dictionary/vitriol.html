Vitriol SUBSTANCE

Vitriolum

Vitriol is one of the most practically important substances in the alchemical laboratory and one of the most symbolically charged terms in the alchemical vocabulary. In chemical terms, vitriol refers to a class of metallic sulfates: green vitriol is ferrous sulfate (iron sulfate), blue vitriol is copper sulfate, and white vitriol is zinc sulfate. These crystalline salts, which form naturally in mines where sulfide ores oxidize, were among the first industrial chemicals, used in metalworking, dyeing, and the production of acids. Green vitriol, when heated, decomposes to produce sulfuric acid (oil of vitriol), one of the most powerful solvents known to early modern chemistry. The alchemists prized vitriol for its caustic, dissolving power, which made it essential for the preliminary operations of the work.

The alchemical significance of vitriol extends far beyond its chemical utility. The word became the basis for one of the most famous alchemical acronyms: V.I.T.R.I.O.L., standing for Visita Interiora Terrae Rectificando Invenies Occultum Lapidem ('Visit the interior of the earth; by rectifying you will find the hidden stone'). This formula, inscribed on the famous engraving in Heinrich Khunrath's Amphitheatrum Sapientiae Aeternae, encapsulates the entire alchemical program: the adept must descend into the depths of matter (and of the self), purify what is found there, and emerge with the philosopher's stone. The word vitriol thus functions as a mnemonic for the Great Work itself.

In the Atalanta Fugiens, vitriol appears in connection with Emblem XXXVII, whose motto declares that three things suffice for mastery: white smoke (water), the green lion (the ore of Hermes), and stinking water. De Jong traces the green lion, one of the most celebrated symbols in the alchemical bestiary, to the green vitriol. The Rosarium Philosophorum states that the true matter can only be obtained in the green lion, also called Adrop, Azoth, or the green Duenech. De Jong identifies the word 'Duenech' as deriving from an Arabic term meaning vitriol, establishing a chain of identification: green lion equals green Duenech equals green vitriol. The famous Rosarium image of the green lion devouring the sun represents the dissolution of gold in green vitriol, the first and most violent operation of the work.

De Jong further connects vitriol to Emblem XXVIII, where King Duenech sits in a steam bath being purged of black bile by his physician Pharut. The bath in which the king sits is identified by De Jong as a vitriol solution, and the process of purging the melancholy associated with Saturn corresponds to the chemical dissolution of impure metal in acid. The greenness of the vitriol links it to the imagery of fertility and growth that pervades the Rosarium: 'O blessed green color, which generates all things,' the text exclaims, connecting the corrosive acid to the regenerative principle of nature.

The dual nature of vitriol, simultaneously destructive and creative, makes it a perfect emblem for the alchemical principle that dissolution is the precondition for regeneration. The acid that destroys the old form of the metal releases the hidden potential within it, just as the nigredo's decomposition prepares the matter for rebirth. De Jong's source analysis reveals that Maier's deployment of vitriol imagery draws on a deep tradition connecting Arabic mineralogy, Latin alchemical compendia, and Renaissance Hermetic philosophy.

For the lay reader, vitriol offers a concrete point of contact between alchemical symbolism and chemical reality. The green crystalline substance that dissolves metals and produces powerful acids was a real and important chemical. But the alchemists transformed it into something more: a symbol for the entire work of penetrating beneath surfaces, dissolving false forms, and recovering the hidden gold within. The famous acronym V.I.T.R.I.O.L. captures this double meaning perfectly, making a chemical substance into a spiritual instruction.
