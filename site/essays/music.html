Approaches to Music in Maier's Atalanta Fugiens

How Scholars Have Understood the Fifty Fugues

The fifty three-voice fugues of Atalanta Fugiens constitute the most distinctive feature of Maier's 1617 emblem book. No other work in the alchemical or emblematic tradition integrates bespoke musical compositions into every emblem. Yet for centuries these fugues were treated as curiosities rather than objects of serious musical analysis. The New Grove Dictionary of Music dismissed them as 'clumsy,' and prior editors and commentators largely ignored the musical dimension. The modern scholarly engagement with Maier's music begins with Joscelyn Godwin's 1987 edition and has since developed along several distinct interpretive lines: musicological, cosmological, performative, therapeutic, and digital. This essay surveys these approaches, drawing on the specific arguments and discoveries of each contributing scholar.

The Cantus Firmus and Three-Voice Structure

Every emblem in the Atalanta Fugiens is accompanied by a three-voice canon (fuga per canonem). Each voice carries a mythological name from the framing narrative: the first voice to sound is the cantus firmus, designated Pomum morans (the delaying apple); the leading voice is Atalanta fugiens (the dux); and the following voice is Hippomenes sequens (the comes). In 1938, Helen Joy Sleeper identified the cantus firmus tenor as the 'Christe eleison' of Gregorian Mass IV, Cunctipotens genitor — a pre-Reformation liturgical melody that had served as the basis for numerous polyphonic compositions. Because the 'Christe eleison' is sung three times in the liturgy, Maier's epigrams are likewise sung three times in each fuga to complete the six-line text. Each canon must therefore be performed three times through to set all the couplets. In the first forty emblems, the cantus firmus enters first, followed by the dux and comes in strict imitation. In the last ten emblems (XLI–L), Wescott notes 'greater variety and complexity in the parts, especially in the treatment of the cantus firmus,' departing from the uniform pattern of the earlier pieces.

Godwin and Streich: The First Modern Edition (1987–1989)

Joscelyn Godwin's edition, published by Phanes Press in 1989, included the first modern transcription of all fifty fugues into standard notation and the first complete recording, performed under the direction of Richard Wistreich with Emily Van Evera as principal singer. Godwin made sensible corrections and emendations to the error-ridden original and completed the missing text underlay. The accompanying seventy-page introductory essay by Hildemarie Streich analyzed the musical structure through the lens of Jungian psychology and Hermetic cosmology. Streich interpreted the beginning tone of the cantus firmus (D) and the final tone (G) as representing the Sun and Saturn respectively, symbolizing the journey of the Alchemical Stone from the spiritual realm to the earthly sphere. She argued that the intervallic relationship reveals the cantus firmus to be in the Dorian mode (normally found at D) transposed into the Mixolydian mode at G, resulting in an interpenetration of solar and Saturnian principles. Godwin himself observed that the canons inhabit a musical world 'almost entirely devoid of the reference points that one expects in music of this period, as close to timelessness as any style could be,' noting that Maier deliberately set aside the rules of counterpoint, including prohibitions of consecutive fifths and octaves and of unprepared dissonances. This stylistic strangeness, Godwin suggested, evokes echoes ranging from Ars Antiqua motets and Machaut to Stravinsky and Hindemith.

Leedy: Musicological Assessment (1991)

Douglas Leedy's review in Notes (Vol. 47, No. 3, March 1991) provided the first sustained evaluation by a professional musicologist. Leedy, a specialist in early music and alternative tuning systems based in Oceanside, Oregon, characterized the Atalanta Fugiens as a Gesamtkunstwerk — a total artwork that augments the communicative power of the traditional word-and-picture emblem book by adding the audible dimension of music. He praised the recording for achieving 'the proper balance of the sensual and the ethereal' but criticized the poor quality of the emblem reproductions compared to the 1964 Barenreiter facsimile. Leedy objected to Streich's discussion of the music as superficial, citing her inadequate explanation of mensuration and proportion and her repeated misidentification of half notes as 'crotchets.' Most importantly, Leedy challenged the dismissive consensus. He noted that the canons are technically ambitious — 'canons at the second, third, fourth, fifth, sixth, and seventh; retrograde, inversion, and mensuration canons' — and argued that their clashes, counterpoint 'mistakes,' and eerie harmonies may result from a technical skill incommensurate with the boldness of the musical conception. He also insisted on the correct modern term: these pieces are canons, not fugues, despite Maier's original designation 'fuga.' Listening to them, Leedy wrote, 'one seems at times to be hearing Machaut, at times Gesualdo or Stravinsky,' and the result has 'a haunting power much like that of Atalanta's engravings.' He further noted that the canons are not truly archaic but comparable to the cantus-firmus canons of contemporary German composers such as Seth Calvisius and Samuel Scheidt.

Wescott: Planetary-Modal Text-Painting

Catherine Morris Wescott's analysis in the AthanorX journal represents the most technically detailed examination of the fugues' modal structure, focusing specifically on four emblems depicting the Alchemical King undergoing Putrefaction and Albification: Emblems XXIV, XXVIII, XXXI, and XLIV. Wescott maps the Renaissance system of planetary-modal correspondences onto Maier's compositions: Saturn governs the Mixolydian mode (final on G), Jupiter the Lydian (final on F), Mars the Phrygian (final on E), the Sun the Dorian (final on D), Venus the Hypolydian (final on C), Mercury the Hypophrygian (final on B), and the Moon the Hypodorian (final on A). Each mode carries allegorical connotations — Saturnian Mixolydian signifies idleness and intellectual pursuit, Martian Phrygian is violent, Venusian Hypolydian represents happiness and feasting, and the Solar Dorian represents theology and the Alchemical Stone. In Emblem XXIV, the fuga begins with the Atalanta voice sounding a Dorian fourth centered on D; in the second measure, the B-natural in the Hippomenes voice signals a shift to C; by measure seven the music gravitates to Dorian at G; and the final two measures resolve on a C major chord, representing the King's Albification in the Venusian Hypolydian. In Emblem XXVIII, the Atalanta voice enters at G while the Hippomenes imitates at A, with E-flat and B-flat creating cross-relations that represent the King's Saturnian melancholy in the steam-bath. In Emblem XXXI, where the naked King struggles in the water, all three voices are treble with no bass to provide stability — a scoring decision that reinforces the image of the King's desperate struggle. The piece ends in the Saturnian Mixolydian, calling for further purification. In Emblem XLIV (the Osiris dismemberment), the comes voice moves in triple time, echoing the tripartite action of dismemberment in the image, and the music resolves unexpectedly in Mixolydian Saturn rather than the expected C triad, reinforcing the cyclical nature of the alchemical process. Wescott also identifies specific moments of text-painting: in Emblem XXIV, the word 'Vulcanus' is set with extended note values in measure five, and the minor second between E-flat and E-natural in measures one through two creates a dissonance 'particularly dreadful to Renaissance ears.'

Hasler: The Performative Argument (2011)

Johann F. W. Hasler's article situates the Atalanta Fugiens within the history of speculative music and argues that it 'requires a performative attitude and activity (in the form of singing) and not merely to be read, for its original purpose to be fully accomplished.' Hasler identifies the work as a genuine precursor to modern multimedia, not merely a book with illustrations and music attached but an integrated multi-sensory experience designed for simultaneous engagement of sight, sound, and intellect. Maier himself instructs that the emblems are 'to be looked at, read, meditated, understood, weighed, sung and listened to, not without a certain pleasure.' Hasler connects the AF to the tradition of Gesprächspiele (conversation games) played in Renaissance academies, citing Godwin's speculation that 'perhaps one should imagine Atalanta being used in a similar way, in the Hermetic academy of Rudolf II's court: an emblem is shown, its descriptive epigram is sung as a three-part fugue, and the participants make their own discourse on its meaning.' Hasler further notes that Maier never explains the musical subtleties in his preface — the reader must figure them out by examining the score. He observes that the parts are printed separately (as orchestral parts, not as a choral score), with the clefs changing between canons, indicating that the vocal ranges interchange the parts of Atalanta and Hippomenes, altering the timbre and sonority of each piece. Hasler also connects the AF to the Balet comique de la Reine (1581) as a courtly performance context in which bodily enactment of harmony served political and therapeutic ends during the French Wars of Religion.

Long: Music, Neuroscience, and Embodied Cognition (2012)

Kathleen Perry Long's article in Lo Sguardo (2012) brings neuroscientific perspectives into dialogue with Maier's alchemical program. Drawing on Daniel Levitin's This Is Your Brain on Music (2006) and Antonio Damasio's work on somatic markers, Long argues that the AF's multi-sensory design — singing in three parts while contemplating a visual emblem — would 'stimulate almost every region of the brain,' from the cochlear nuclei and brain stem to the auditory cortices, visual cortex, language centers, and the amygdala. She traces the tradition of music as therapy from antiquity through Marsilio Ficino, whose discussions of music's healing properties were foundational for the early modern understanding of music in medicine. Long proposes that Maier was practicing 'an alchemy of the brain' long before the functions of the brain were understood, combining meditative and aesthetic practices with images of kingship focused on harmony in order to shape his patron's thinking. She notes that each performance of a fuga lasts roughly ninety seconds, during which the body as well as the mind is engaged with the text — a form of complex meditation that engages 'somatic markers' of the kind Damasio identifies as essential to effective reasoning. Long connects the musical-meditative program to the tradition of Ignatian spiritual exercises, arguing that the AF augments existing meditative practices (focused visualization combined with repeated phrases or songs) with a musical component that intensifies the bodily engagement. She also suggests that the social aspect of group performance — the constant decision-making, attention to other performers, and coordination required — may have been therapeutically significant in a culture Damasio would call 'sick,' where decades of religious warfare had derailed healthy affective reasoning.

Forshaw: The Quadrivium Framework (2012, 2020)

Peter Forshaw's Infinite Fire webinar and his essay in the Furnace and Fugue edition reveal that Maier structures each emblem's discourse around the four disciplines of the medieval quadrivium: arithmetic, music, geometry, and astronomy. In Emblem I, for example, Maier provides four parallel encodings of the alchemical concept: arithmetically, the philosopher's stone is 'the root of the cube'; musically, it is the disdiapason or double octave; geometrically, it is 'a generative point of a flowing line'; and astronomically, it is 'the center of Saturn, Jupiter, and Mars.' Music is thus not an addition to the emblem but one of four parallel mathematical languages for expressing the same alchemical truth. Forshaw notes that the three-voice structure recurs in Maier's later work Cantilenae Intellectuales (c. 1622), suggesting a sustained interest in triadic musical form. He also highlights that Thomas Norton's Ordinall of Alchemy (1477), which Maier translated into German during his years in England (1612–1616), stresses the interconnections between musical intervals, alchemical proportions, planetary influence, and the soul of the practitioner. Forshaw points to Maier's article 'Laboratorium, Auditorium, Oratorium' as an important discussion of the relationship between alchemical music and the spaces — laboratory, lecture hall, and chapel — in which it functioned.

Ludwig: The John Farmer Discovery (2020)

The most groundbreaking revelation to emerge from the Furnace and Fugue project is Loren Ludwig's demonstration that forty of the fifty fugues were copied from John Farmer's Divers and Sundry Waies of Two Parts in One (1591), an English publication of two-part canons. Ludwig's discovery, published as the essay 'John Farmer's Sundry Waies: The English Origin of Michael Maier's Alchemical Fugues' in the Furnace and Fugue edition (2020), transformed understanding of Maier's compositional method: he was primarily an arranger and adapter, selecting pre-existing canonic material from an English crypto-Catholic liturgical tradition and fitting it to his alchemical program by adding a third voice (the cantus firmus) and new Latin text underlay. This discovery places the Atalanta Fugiens in the reception line of English sacred polyphony and raises questions about the ten remaining fugues (XLI–L), which show greater compositional independence. Whether Maier composed these final ten himself or drew on other unidentified sources remains an open question. As the RIDE review by Sarah Lang observes, Ludwig's finding 'raises questions about the role of the fugues in Maier's overall work,' since a crypto-Catholic intention does not align with Maier's otherwise Lutheran faith, yet the reuse of these canons underscores his belief in music as a transmission medium for secret knowledge.

Bianchi: Silent Contemplation and Courtly Knowledge (2020)

Eric Bianchi's essay in Furnace and Fugue, titled 'Weight, Number, Measure: The Musical Universe of Michael Maier,' offers a counterpoint to the performative reading of the fugues. Bianchi argues that the Atalanta Fugiens is unlikely to have been intended for actual vocal performance but rather as an invitation to silent contemplation in the context of contemporary music theory — an ideal that Maier later realized more explicitly in his Cantilenae Intellectuales (1622). Bianchi notes that in Maier's social milieu, composing music and possessing theoretical knowledge of music theory were markers of belonging to the courtly elite (the intended audience for the emblem book), while actually playing music was the task of skilled artisans. This distinction between musica theorica and musica practica suggests that the fugues may have operated primarily as intellectual objects — puzzles to be solved by eye rather than ear, demonstrations of learning rather than invitations to sing. Bianchi's reading complicates the claims of Hasler and Long by suggesting that the AF's music may have been designed to function even without performance, as a purely notational encoding of harmonic relationships that the educated reader could apprehend through visual inspection of the score.

Recordings and Modern Performance

Despite the debate over whether Maier intended actual performance, the fugues have been realized in sound by several ensembles since Godwin's 1987 cassette recording. The Wistreich-directed recording included with Godwin's edition was praised by Leedy for its balance of the sensual and the ethereal. Ensemble Plus Ultra under Michael Noone produced a complete recording (released in conjunction with a 2006 Atalanta Fugiens conference in Philadelphia), and the Vagantes A.V. Consort recorded the canons in 2006. The Furnace and Fugue digital edition (2020) includes an MEI (Music Encoding Initiative) player with a piano-roll visualization, allowing users without musical training to see the contrapuntal structure of the fugues in real time — a technological realization of the multi-sensory engagement Maier described. These performances and visualizations have made it possible for modern audiences to experience what Leedy called the 'haunting power' of the canons and to form their own judgment about whether the clashes and eerie harmonies are the product of deliberate text-painting (as Wescott argues) or of a bold but imperfect compositional technique.

Synthesis: Music as Alchemical Medium

Taken together, these scholarly approaches reveal the fugues as far more than decorative additions to an emblem book. Wescott demonstrates that Maier's modal choices encode planetary-alchemical symbolism at a technically specific level, with chromatic shifts and modal transpositions functioning as musical text-painting. Long and Hasler argue that the act of singing engages the body in a form of embodied cognition that exceeds what visual contemplation alone could achieve. Forshaw shows that the music participates in a quadrivial structure that organizes every emblem around the four mathematical disciplines. Ludwig's Farmer discovery reveals that Maier selected his musical material with care from the English polyphonic tradition, adapting it to serve his alchemical program. And Bianchi reminds us that the boundary between performance and contemplation was itself unstable in Maier's intellectual world. Whether the fugues were sung aloud in the Hermetic academy of Rudolf II's court, contemplated silently by a solitary reader who could mentally reconstruct the three voices, or analyzed as notational puzzles by a musically literate courtier, they represent Maier's most ambitious attempt to join sight and hearing with the intellect — to create, as he wrote, a portal through which the secrets of nature might enter the soul.
