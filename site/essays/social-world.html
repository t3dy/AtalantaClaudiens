Maier's Social World

Courts, Patrons, Associates, and Alchemical Movements

Maier's Social World: Patronage, Friendship, and Intellectual Community in Early Seventeenth-Century Europe

Michael Maier was born in the summer of 1569 in the vicinity of Kiel, in staunchly Lutheran Holstein, then an ethnically German province under Danish rule on the northern border of the Holy Roman Empire. His father Peter was a Goldsticker, an embroiderer in gold and pearls, who served the Danish royalty and nobility, including King Friedrich II of Denmark and the powerful governor of all Schleswig-Holstein, Heinrich Rantzau (1526–1598). The elder Maier's professional concern with gold—the most noble of metals and a symbol of divine and kingly power—was, as Hereward Tilton observes, a portent of things to come for his son. Peter was not content that Michael should learn his handicraft; though it had proved lucrative, it was manual labor and lacked the prestige needed for social advancement into the nobility. From the age of five, Michael was educated in the literary arts, studying not only the usual Trivium but also music and poetry. When his father sometimes threatened to consign him to an artisan's trade, the boy would burst into tears.

The Dove Augury and the Alchemical Vocation

In his autobiographical first chapter of De Medicina Regia (Prague, 1609)—a document recently uncovered by Karin Figala and Ulrich Neumann at the Royal Library in Copenhagen—Maier relates a remarkable tale of augury presaging his birth. Three days before he was born, his mother was sojourning in the countryside with a relative. Sitting in the grass while the relative went away, a dove flew unbidden into her lap. Marvelling at the bird's beauty and tameness, she tried to catch it, but it withdrew and flew away. Maier presents the episode with studied ambivalence, but as Tilton argues, the dove was a standard alchemical motif linking the Christian symbolism of the Holy Spirit to the spiritus ascending and descending within the alchemical vessel during distillation. By juxtaposing this tale with an account of the preparation of the Philosophical Mercury, Maier hinted at a correspondence between the alchemical process, his own life, and the myth of Christ—likening the alchemical vessel to his mother's womb.

From Rostock to Padua: The Making of a Scholar

Peter Maier died before 1587, and Michael was sent at the expense of a maternal relative to the University of Rostock to study philosophy and the liberal arts. His earliest known publication dates from this period: a letter to Heinrich Rantzau, printed in an edition of 100 copies from Rostock on 18 June 1590. Between 1589 and 1591 Maier journeyed to Padua, where he received the title of Poet Laureate. In 1592 he obtained the Doctorate of Philosophy from the University of Frankfurt an der Oder, and in 1596 the degree of Doctor of Medicine from the University of Basel. His dissertation, De epilepsia, is lost; no copy is known to survive.

The Padua years harbored a darker episode. In July 1596, the twenty-seven-year-old Maier attacked and seriously wounded a fellow scholar named Heino Lambechius following verbal disputes. The annals of the German Nation at Padua describe the assault as unprecedented. Maier was tried, judged guilty, ordered to pay expenses, and compelled to deliver a plea for forgiveness before the elders. The next day, he fled Padua in secret when Lambechius refused monetary compensation. When Maier arrived at Basel to complete his doctorate, the German Nation sent a warrant-like letter demanding he not be permitted to graduate. Fortunately for Maier, he defended his theses successfully on 4 November 1596, but the incident left a mark—his struggle with anger, combined with his precarious social position, drove his later quest for an alchemical medicine that might cure both body and spirit.

The Alchemical Laboratory in Kiel

Shortly after returning to Holstein to practice medicine, Maier witnessed a remarkable cure effected through alchemy, which reoriented him entirely. With characteristic systematic discipline, he first compiled a glossary of alchemical terms, studied theory by sifting good authors from bad, then undertook first-hand investigation of natural phenomena in mining regions. Finally he set up a laboratory in Kiel with his brother-in-law (his sister's husband; Maier never married). His alchemical work lasted from 1602 until 1607 or 1608. In a letter to Fürst August von Anhalt-Plötzkau dated 5 August 1610—discovered by Dr. Carlos Gilly in the town archive of Oranienbaum, Russia—Maier made the extraordinary claim that he had obtained the Universal Medicine, describing it as being of a bright lemon color. He was unable to proceed further owing to technical difficulties and financial exhaustion. His avocation had also made him an object of hostile curiosity among the provincial burghers of Kiel.

The Court of Rudolf II: Prague, 1608–1611

Under such conditions, where else would a three-quarters successful alchemist turn but to the court of the Holy Roman Emperor? Around mid-1608, Maier went to Prague and presented himself at the Hradčany Palace. Rudolf II had assembled a remarkable entourage of alchemists, Kabbalists, magicians, astronomers, poets, and artists from across Europe—among them the astronomers Tycho Brahe and Johannes Kepler, the alchemists Oswald Croll and Martin Ruland, and occasional visitors including John Dee and Giordano Bruno. Rudolf welcomed learned Catholics, Lutherans, and Calvinists alike, fostering a spirit of cross-confessional tolerance and the pursuit of pansophia, a universal science connecting microcosm and macrocosm.

It took Maier about a year to penetrate the circles of courtly obstruction surrounding the reclusive Emperor. The door may have been opened by publication of his De Medicina Regia, which served as both a curriculum vitae and a petition for patronage. On 19 September 1609 he entered the Emperor's service, and ten days later was raised to the nobility. His portrait lists, alongside his academic honors, the three titles Rudolf gave him: Personal Physician, Count Palatine, and Knight Exemptus. Others so honored at Rudolf's court included the diplomat Johann Mathias Wacker von Wackenfels, the painter Giuseppe Arcimboldo, and the alchemists Johann Müller von Müllenfels and Sir Edward Kelley.

The Alchemical Coat of Arms

Maier's ennoblement was accompanied by a deeply personal symbol of his threefold identity. The escutcheon beside his portrait—which he petitioned the Emperor to grant—shows a laurel branch from which three twigs spring, and an eagle flying upward, a symbol taken directly from alchemy. The eagle is checked in its flight by a toad crawling along the soil, the two creatures connected by a golden chain. As De Jong explains, the eagle represents the volatile spirit and the toad the fixed, earthy principle—together they symbolize the majestic conjunction of opposites at the heart of the alchemical work. Maier derived this emblem from Avicenna by way of the Symbola Aureae Mensae, where the Arab sage stands with eagle and toad bound by a golden chain. The coat of arms is crowned by a repetition of the laurel branch and by an eagle with a closed ring in its beak, symbolizing eternity and the circular motion of the universe. As Peter Forshaw notes, the fact that Maier chose an explicitly alchemical coat of arms testifies to how thoroughly his identity as physician, nobleman, and alchemist had become fused.

England, 1611–1616: Diplomatic Mission and Hermetic Circles

Maier's golden years in Prague were brief. Rudolf was forced to abdicate in April 1611 in favor of his brother Matthias, and died a virtual prisoner in his own palace on 20 January 1612. According to Ulrich Neumann, Maier turned to three alchemically inclined rulers: August von Anhalt-Plötzkau, Moritz von Hessen-Kassel, and Ernst III von Holstein-Schauenburg. But instead of settling with any of them, Maier departed for England, arriving before Christmas 1611. Godwin argues that his nearly five years there were almost certainly the fulfillment of a diplomatic mission, preparing the ground for the dynastic marriage of Elector Frederick V of the Palatinate and Princess Elizabeth, daughter of King James I. Maier's status as a Count Palatine and familiar of Rudolf made him an acceptable envoy, while his contacts with three German Protestant rulers suggest he was working for the coalition forming against an aggressively Catholic Habsburg monarchy.

Maier announced his presence in England with a most unusual visiting card: a Christmas greeting to King James I, now preserved in the Scottish Record Office in Edinburgh (GD 242/212). It was a folded parchment measuring 33 by 24 inches, on which a central Rose-Cross emblem made from words in gold and red was flanked by four Latin poems and a musical canon in six parts representing the songs of angels and shepherds. Godwin identifies this as the earliest known appearance of the Rose-Cross symbol in England. The following year, Maier prepared a second Christmas greeting for the 18-year-old Henry, Prince of Wales—an oversized parchment (now British Library, Royal MSS 14.B.xvi) composed entirely of words arranged in geometric shapes forming acrostics. Alas, Prince Henry died of typhoid fever on 6 November 1612 before receiving it.

English Associates: Paddy, Anthony, Fludd, and Smith

In London, Maier frequented a circle of Hermetic physicians close to the Court. The man he definitely knew was Sir William Paddy, James I's personal physician, to whom he dedicated his Arcana arcanissima (1614), his first alchemical publication from the English period. Paddy was a fellow of St John's College, Oxford, a graduate in medicine of Leiden, and later President of the College of Physicians of London. He was also a close friend of Robert Fludd, receiving the dedication of Fludd's Medicina Catholica (1629). Frances Yates drew the conclusion that Maier frequented in London the same kind of Hermetic circle that had surrounded Rudolf in Prague, and that he brought to this English circle, three years before the Fama was published, news of the rising movement in Protestant Germany.

Another English friend was Francis Anthony, a Cambridge graduate and son of a London goldsmith who had caused considerable stir by claiming to have discovered Aurum Potabile, drinkable gold. Maier dedicated his Lusus Serius (1616) to Anthony and two other friends, describing them in the dedication as wise doctors of medicine, expert chemists, and his most jocund companions. Craven also records Maier's friendship with Sir Thomas Smith, a figure so much in King James's favor that he had been sent as ambassador to the Emperor of Russia; Smith was the first governor of the East India Company, treasurer of the Virginia Company, and the prime undertaker for the discovery of the Northwest Passage. A copy of Arcana arcanissima bearing a manuscript dedication in what is believed to be Maier's own hand was inscribed to Smith. Whether Maier actually met Robert Fludd remains debated—there is no documentary proof, though it strains credulity to imagine they never crossed paths, given their shared publisher (Matthias Merian, who engraved both the Atalanta Fugiens and Fludd's Utriusque cosmi historia) and their common friend Paddy.

Eleven Books in Two Years: The Frankfurt Explosion, 1616–1618

Maier must have devoted much of his English sojourn to research and writing, for within two years of his return to Germany in mid-1616 he published eleven books—an astonishing output that Godwin catalogs in full. They were: De circulo physico quadrato (dedicated to Moritz von Hessen, August 1616); Lusus serius (dedicated to Francis Anthony and others, September 1616); Examen fucorum pseudo-chymicorum (dedicated to Joachim Hirschberger, September 1616); Jocus severus (dedicated to German lovers of chemistry and the Brethren of the Rose-Cross); Silentium post clamores (issued anonymously, 1617); Symbola aureae mensae (dedicated to Ernst III von Holstein-Schauenburg, December 1616); Atalanta fugiens (dedicated to Christoph Reinhart of Mühlhausen, August 1617); Tripus aureus (dedicated to Johannes Hartmann Beyer, January 1618); Themis aurea (no dedication, 1618); Viatorium (dedicated to Christian von Anhalt, September 1618); and Verum inventum (dedicated to the Town Councillors of Frankfurt, September 1618). His publishers were Lucas Jennis and Johann Theodor de Bry in Frankfurt and Oppenheim. He lived for two years in Frankfurt am Main, perhaps partly supported by these publishers, and dedicated his books either to fellow scholars or to Hermetically inclined Protestant rulers.

The Unlanded Nobleman's Predicament

A word must be said about the social and economic predicament that drove this relentless production. Born the son of a prosperous craftsman, Maier had been made a Count Palatine and Knight Exemptus. But Rudolf had not thought to add a grant of land that would provide income commensurate with such titles. Maier was henceforth barred from modest employment such as tutoring or general medical practice, yet possessed no property or inherited wealth. Godwin summarizes the situation bluntly: Maier was forced into a kind of upper-class beggary, dependent on attachment to some greater nobleman's household or on diplomatic status. The carefully chosen dedications of his books were not mere courtesies but essential strategies for survival—each one addressed to a potential patron whose alchemical or Hermetic sympathies Maier had identified.

Moritz von Hessen-Kassel and the Rosicrucian Moment

In 1618 Maier was rewarded by Moritz von Hessen-Kassel—the foremost patron of alchemy in the German states—with the official title of Medicus und Chymicus von Hauss aus, that is, Original Physician and Alchemist. Moritz was a formidable humanist scholar who had promoted the first ever university professor of chemical medicine, Johannes Hartmann, appointed at the University of Marburg in 1609. As Forshaw emphasizes, Moritz's lands were also the location of the Rosicrucian publications: the Fama Fraternitatis (1614), the Confessio (1615), and the Chemical Wedding (1616). Maier had paid little attention to the Rosicrucian excitement while in England, but once at Moritz's court he became fascinated, and his literary output turned increasingly toward defense and promotion of the Brotherhood.

Maier's Rosicrucian engagement took several forms. His Silentium post clamores—his sole book in German, evidently aimed at a popular audience—defended the Brethren for their silence after publishing the Fama and Confessio. His Themis aurea, one of only two of his works translated into English in the seventeenth century, argued in favor of the Brethren's rules: keeping anonymity, healing the sick without charge. His Jocus severus was dedicated directly to the Rose-Cross. Yet Maier never claimed membership in the Brotherhood. As Godwin argues, Maier accepted that the Brethren did not make themselves known, believing this the best strategy for an imperfect world. He was a living proof of their wisdom—a mind of exceptional caliber devoting himself to the cause without personal contact or initiation.

German Patriotism and the Europe-as-Woman Conceit

Maier was strongly conscious of being German and proud of it, and his journey to England only intensified his patriotism. In the Symbola aureae mensae, in the chapter on English alchemy, he celebrates the generous hospitality he enjoyed in England but records being much offended by English actors who in their public plays introduced Germans as drunk and babbling, mixing in Teutonic words and showing German women as near-beasts and the Emperor as a petty prince. In turn, Maier criticized the English as barbaric pronouncers of Latin and even of their own language. In Verum inventum, his patriotic work, the Rosicrucian movement appears as the crown of Germany's gifts to humankind.

In the Septimana philosophica (1620), Maier illustrated Europe as a female figure and elaborated the conceit in his characteristic semi-scientific, semi-emblematic manner. The shape people see on the face of the moon, he argued, is the reflection of the woman-shaped continent of Europe, caused by sunlight striking the earth's surface and sending the image back to the moon's perfectly smooth surface (this was before Galileo's telescopic discoveries). Maier added that Germany is Europe's belly, and that Germany is pregnant with great things for the future—an allusion to the Rosicrucian manifestos. Through this image, astronomy merged into sacred geography, and German nationalism into cosmic destiny.

The Twelve Mystery Schools and the Perennial Philosophy

The Rosicrucian Brotherhood found its place within Maier's overarching intellectual framework: a belief in twelve mystery schools appearing chronologically in twelve nations, from Hermes Trismegistus in ancient Egypt through the Hebrews, Greeks, Romans, Arabians, and the various European nations, culminating in the Rosicrucian Brotherhood in modern Germany. This architecture is most fully elaborated in the Symbola aureae mensae, organized around a banquet for the Virgin Alchemy attended by one representative adept from each of twelve national traditions. Godwin notes that although Maier never uses the phrase philosophia perennis, he obviously conceived of a perennial wisdom handed down from ancient days and manifesting in pagan and Abrahamic religions alike. The Brotherhood was the latest and greatest link in this golden chain.

Ornithology, the Barnacle Goose, and Fortean Wonders

Forshaw draws attention to a quality that sets Maier apart from many contemporaries: his singular fascination with birds. Birds pervade alchemical symbolism—winged birds symbolize volatile spirits ascending in the flask, wingless birds the fixed sediment—but Maier took this further than most. His Jocus severus stages a court case among birds, with the Phoenix as judge and the Raven as prosecutor against the Owl (the bird of Pallas Athena), who is ultimately vindicated. His Tractatus de volucri arborea (1619) is devoted to the barnacle goose, a creature Maier claimed to have seen in Scotland during his British years. The barnacle goose—a large barnacle hanging from rotting timber that was believed to transform into a goose—represented to Maier an example of spontaneous generation, hence of virgin birth, hence an emblem of Christ. As Godwin notes, Maier was a kind of Fortean, fascinated by anomalies and wonders that the science of his day could not yet classify.

Last Years: Magdeburg and Death, 1620–1622

There is no evidence of why Maier left Moritz's household after only two years, except the obvious political circumstances. The death of Emperor Matthias, the rebellion of the Bohemian estates, the catastrophic Battle of the White Mountain (1620)—all these weighed heavily on Moritz and threatened his realm. Already pressed for military funds, Moritz had neither time nor money for esoteric pursuits. In 1620 Maier appeared in Magdeburg, where he had a potential patron in the Markgraf Christian Wilhelm von Brandenburg. In 1622 he petitioned Herzog Friedrich III von Schleswig-Holstein-Gottorf, apparently hoping to return to his Baltic homeland. But his plans never matured. Maier died in Magdeburg in the late summer of 1622. His friend who published the posthumous Ulysses (1624) recorded that Maier passed away piously, was a regular attendant at the house of God, a Christian in life and conversation, and practiced charity as shown in the parable of the Good Samaritan.

The Ulysses, a forty-page essay published after Maier's death, sounded a valedictory note imbued with Christian Stoicism. It treats of how to recover from the shipwreck of bodily goods and fortune through the virtues of Ulysses—that is, through the intellect. All records of Maier's burial were destroyed when Magdeburg was sacked by Tilly's forces in 1631; nearly the entire population of 30,000 perished by fire, sword, or drowning in the Elbe. The cathedral authorities have confirmed that, so far as they know, Maier was not buried there, and no trace of his tomb survives.

Maier's travels had taken him as far south as Padua, as far west as London, through all the states of Germany and beyond the borders of the Swedish empire to Russian-controlled Ivangorod. He conceived of Europe as an entity and grasped the external world through traditional cosmology, Hermetic concepts, and his own restless experience of the continent. His life was governed by what Tilton calls a spirit of paradox in keeping with the central motif of the alchemical opus, the coniunctio oppositorum—for it was precisely his lifelong toil to procure the Universal Medicine that constituted the nigredo phase of his own spiritual alchemy. In the Allegoria Bella appended to the Symbola Aureae Mensae, Maier wrote that in the chemistry of the soul, there is a splendid substance passed from master to master, in whose beginning lies misery with vinegar, but in whose end lies joy with gladness.
