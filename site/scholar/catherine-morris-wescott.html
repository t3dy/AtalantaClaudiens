Catherine Morris Wescott

Music and Alchemy

Modal analysis of Maier's fugues as text-painting for alchemical Putrefaction and Albification

Catherine Morris Wescott is a scholar of music and alchemy whose analysis of the Atalanta Fugiens fugues, published in the AthanorX journal, provides the most technically detailed examination of Maier's musical compositions in our corpus. Her work uniquely combines musicological analysis with alchemical interpretation, reading the fifty three-voice fugues not merely as musical settings of the epigram texts but as sonic encodings of alchemical processes.

Wescott's approach brings together music theory (modal analysis, interval identification, cantus firmus technique, text-painting) with the alchemical symbolism of the emblem tradition. She demonstrates that Maier's modal choices — Dorian, Phrygian, Mixolydian, and others — correspond to planetary associations established in classical and medieval music theory (Saturn, Mars, Jupiter) that in turn relate to the specific alchemical stages and metals depicted in each emblem's plate. This systematic correspondence between mode, planet, and alchemical operation suggests a level of compositional intentionality that goes beyond conventional text-setting.

Wescott's most detailed analyses concern Emblems XXIV, XXVIII, XXXI, and XLIV, where she combines musical and iconographic readings to show how the three voices of each fugue — typically representing Atalanta (soprano), Hippomenes (tenor), and the golden apple (cantus firmus) — enact the alchemical drama of pursuit, fixation, and transformation. She identifies the cantus firmus as the structural anchor derived from liturgical chant, around which the two pursuing voices weave their contrapuntal dialogue. Her analysis of the planetary-modal correspondences in these emblems reveals how the musical dimension reinforces the visual and textual content rather than merely accompanying it.

For this site, Wescott provides fifteen scholarly references spanning both musical and alchemical interpretation. Her work is essential for anyone seeking to understand the fugues as integral components of Maier's emblematic program rather than decorative additions. Together with Godwin's edition and Leedy's review, Wescott's analysis forms the core of the site's engagement with the musical dimension of the Atalanta Fugiens.
