H.M.E. de Jong

Art history, source criticism, alchemical iconography

Source identification for all 50 emblems; meaning, sources, and interconnections

H.M.E. de Jong is a Dutch art historian whose 1969 dissertation, Michael Maier's Atalanta fugiens: Sources of an Alchemical Book of Emblems, remains the foundational work in Atalanta Fugiens scholarship. Her method is rigorously source-critical: she systematically identifies the ancient and medieval texts from which Maier drew each emblem's motto, imagery, and doctrinal content, revealing the Atalanta Fugiens as a work of sophisticated synthesis rather than original invention. De Jong demonstrates that the motto of each emblem can be traced to specific textual antecedents in the Turba Philosophorum, the Rosarium Philosophorum, the Tabula Smaragdina, Pseudo-Aristotle, and other alchemical compilations. Her interpretive framework treats the motto as the primary key to each emblem, with the engraved plate, epigram, and discourse functioning as successive layers of elaboration. She further maps the emblem sequence onto the four classical stages of the alchemical opus -- nigredo, albedo, citrinitas, and rubedo -- establishing a pedagogical logic that runs through the entire work. Her earlier 1964 article on Emblem XLVIII, which traces the Merlini allegory through the King Duenech narrative, exemplifies the depth of her emblem-by-emblem analysis. De Jong's source identifications provide the interpretive skeleton upon which all subsequent Atalanta Fugiens scholarship builds.
